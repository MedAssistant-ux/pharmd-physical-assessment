# Episode Three. Skin, Hair, and Nails.

[pause=1]

Welcome back. The skin is the largest organ in the body, and for the pharmacist, it's also the most readable. Roughly two to three percent of all hospitalized patients develop a cutaneous adverse drug reaction. Most are mild. A small but critical fraction are life-threatening. Recognizing the difference can save a patient's life, and that recognition starts with vocabulary.

[pause=1]

By the end of this episode you'll know how to describe a lesion precisely, identify the major drug eruption patterns, distinguish dangerous reactions from benign ones, and read hair and nail findings as windows into systemic disease.

[pause=2]

## Learning Objectives

[pause=1]

One. Describe a skin lesion using accurate morphologic vocabulary: macule, papule, plaque, vesicle, bulla, pustule, wheal, nodule.

Two. Identify the common drug eruption patterns and the typical timing of each.

Three. Distinguish severe cutaneous adverse reactions: SJS, TEN, DRESS, and AGEP.

Four. Recognize common hair loss patterns and drug-induced alopecia.

Five. Read nail findings for systemic clues: clubbing, koilonychia, Beau's lines, half-and-half nails.

[pause=2]

## The Inspection of Skin

[pause=1]

Before you describe anything, set yourself up to see it. Good light. Natural daylight is best. Tangential light, where light strikes the skin at an angle, reveals subtle elevations and depressions. A dermatoscope or even a smartphone macro lens is useful.

[pause=1]

Inspect the full skin surface as the patient is willing. Don't forget the scalp, the back, between the toes, the palms and soles, the mucous membranes, and the genital area when relevant and consented to. Many drug eruptions and skin cancers hide in plain sight on areas you don't routinely expose.

[pause=2]

## Lesion Morphology: The Vocabulary

[pause=1]

You cannot describe a rash you don't have the words for. Memorize these.

[pause=1]

Macule. A flat lesion, less than one centimeter, with a color change but no elevation. A freckle is a macule. The rash of measles starts as macules.

[pause=1]

Patch. The same as a macule but larger than one centimeter. Vitiligo is a patch.

[pause=1]

Papule. A raised lesion less than one centimeter. An early acne bump is a papule.

[pause=1]

Plaque. A raised lesion larger than one centimeter, broader than it is thick. Psoriasis is the classic plaque.

[pause=1]

Nodule. A deeper, firmer lesion, often extending into the dermis. A rheumatoid nodule.

[pause=1]

Vesicle. A small, less than one centimeter, fluid-filled blister. Shingles vesicles.

[pause=1]

Bulla. A larger, greater than one centimeter, fluid-filled blister. The bullae of pemphigus or severe burns.

[pause=1]

Pustule. A vesicle filled with pus. Acne pustules. The classic pustules of AGEP, a drug reaction we'll come back to.

[pause=1]

Wheal. A transient, raised, edematous lesion. The hives of urticaria.

[pause=1]

Two more words you must own.

Erythema. Redness from increased blood flow, which blanches with pressure.

Purpura. Reddish-purple from extravasated blood in the skin, which does not blanch. Petechiae are small purpura, less than three millimeters. Ecchymoses are larger purpura, the typical bruise. The blanch test, pressing a glass slide or your finger against the lesion, separates erythema from purpura, and that distinction is critical because purpura can signal vasculitis, severe thrombocytopenia, or meningococcal sepsis.

[pause=2]

## Distribution and Pattern

[pause=1]

After morphology, describe distribution. Is the rash generalized or localized? Symmetric or asymmetric? Truncal or acral, meaning on the hands and feet? Photodistributed, meaning on sun-exposed surfaces only? Dermatomal, following a single nerve root distribution like shingles? Annular, ring-shaped? Confluent, where individual lesions merge?

[pause=1]

The combination of morphology plus distribution narrows the differential dramatically. A truncal symmetric macular eruption that started seven days after starting an antibiotic is a different beast than a photodistributed papular eruption on a patient taking doxycycline.

[pause=2]

## The Major Drug Eruption Patterns

[pause=1]

Hundreds of drug reaction patterns exist. As a pharmacist, master these.

[pause=2]

### Morbilliform, also called Maculopapular Drug Eruption

[pause=1]

The most common drug rash, accounting for about ninety percent of cutaneous adverse reactions. It looks like measles: symmetric, truncal, pink to red macules and papules that coalesce, often itchy, typically appearing five to fourteen days after starting a new drug. Common culprits: aminopenicillins, sulfonamides, allopurinol, anticonvulsants. Usually benign and resolves on stopping the drug, but a morbilliform rash that progresses, develops mucosal involvement, or comes with fever and lymphadenopathy may be the harbinger of something worse.

[pause=2]

### Urticaria

[pause=1]

Hives. Pruritic wheals that come and go within twenty-four hours at any given site. Often IgE-mediated, occurring within minutes to hours of drug exposure. Common culprits: penicillins, NSAIDs, opioids, contrast agents. When urticaria comes with angioedema of the lips, tongue, or larynx, or with hypotension and respiratory distress, you are looking at anaphylaxis. That is a now problem.

[pause=2]

### Fixed Drug Eruption

[pause=1]

A solitary or small number of round, dusky red to purple plaques, often with a darker center, that recur in exactly the same location every time the patient is exposed to the offending drug. Common culprits: tetracyclines, NSAIDs, sulfonamides, carbamazepine. Heals with persistent hyperpigmentation. The pearl: ask about prior episodes. Fixed drug eruption is named for its fixed location on rechallenge.

[pause=2]

### Photosensitivity Reactions

[pause=1]

A rash limited to sun-exposed areas: face, ears, V of the neck, dorsa of the hands and forearms. Two flavors. Phototoxic, which looks like an exaggerated sunburn, common with tetracyclines, fluoroquinolones, amiodarone, and thiazides. Photoallergic, which looks more eczematous and may spread beyond sun-exposed areas, common with sulfonamides and chlorpromazine. Counsel patients on sun protection at the time of dispensing.

[pause=2]

### Acneiform Eruption

[pause=1]

Monomorphic papules and pustules, often on the face, chest, and back, without comedones. Look for EGFR inhibitors like cetuximab and erlotinib, lithium, isoniazid, corticosteroids. The presence of comedones, the blackheads and whiteheads, points more to true acne than a drug eruption.

[pause=2]

## Severe Cutaneous Adverse Reactions: The Ones That Kill

[pause=1]

These are the reactions every pharmacist must recognize. If you can name them and call them, you may save a life.

[pause=2]

### Stevens-Johnson Syndrome and Toxic Epidermal Necrolysis

[pause=1]

Same spectrum, different severity, based on body surface area involvement. SJS: less than ten percent. SJS-TEN overlap: ten to thirty percent. TEN: more than thirty percent.

[pause=1]

The clinical picture. A prodrome of fever and flu-like symptoms one to three days before the rash. Then painful, dusky, atypical target lesions starting on the trunk and spreading. Mucosal involvement is the dead giveaway: erosions and ulcers in the mouth, eyes, genitals. The skin sloughs in sheets — the Nikolsky sign is positive, meaning lateral pressure detaches the epidermis. Skin pain often out of proportion to visible findings.

[pause=1]

Timing: typically four to twenty-eight days after starting the drug. Highest-risk drugs: lamotrigine, carbamazepine, allopurinol, sulfonamide antibiotics, nevirapine, oxicam NSAIDs.

[pause=1]

Action: stop the suspect drug immediately, refer to emergency department, anticipate burn-unit-level care, treat fluid loss, anticipate sepsis, ophthalmology consult for ocular involvement.

[pause=2]

### DRESS: Drug Reaction with Eosinophilia and Systemic Symptoms

[pause=1]

A late drug reaction, two to eight weeks after starting the drug. The clinical picture: facial edema, lymphadenopathy, fever, a widespread morbilliform rash that may evolve to erythroderma, and most importantly, internal organ involvement. The liver is most often affected. Eosinophilia and atypical lymphocytes on CBC. Mortality up to ten percent, usually from hepatitis or myocarditis.

[pause=1]

Classic culprits: aromatic anticonvulsants (phenytoin, carbamazepine, lamotrigine, phenobarbital), allopurinol, sulfonamides, vancomycin, minocycline.

[pause=1]

If you see a patient with facial swelling, fever, and a rash four weeks after starting an anticonvulsant, you must think DRESS. Stop the drug and refer.

[pause=2]

### AGEP: Acute Generalized Exanthematous Pustulosis

[pause=1]

A rapid eruption, often within forty-eight hours of starting the drug, of hundreds of small non-follicular pustules on a background of erythema, classically starting in the intertriginous areas, the folds. Fever common. Resolves quickly when the drug is stopped, usually within two weeks. Main culprits: aminopenicillins, calcium channel blockers, antimalarials, antibiotics in general.

[pause=2]

## A Quick Way to Triage a Drug Rash

[pause=1]

When you see a drug rash, ask five questions.

One. Is there mucosal involvement? Mouth, eyes, genitals?

Two. Is the skin painful, not just itchy?

Three. Are there atypical targets, dusky centers, sloughing, positive Nikolsky?

Four. Is there fever, facial edema, lymphadenopathy, or laboratory evidence of organ involvement?

Five. Is the patient sicker than the rash alone would suggest?

[pause=1]

Any one of those is a yellow flag. Multiple is a red flag. Stop the drug, refer.

[pause=2]

## Hair Assessment

[pause=1]

Two patterns of hair loss to recognize and the drug list for each.

[pause=1]

Anagen effluvium. Hair lost during the growth phase, occurring within days to weeks of exposure. The classic cause is cytotoxic chemotherapy: doxorubicin, cyclophosphamide, paclitaxel. Massive loss, often a distinctive "broken hair" look on inspection.

[pause=1]

Telogen effluvium. Hair shifted prematurely into the resting phase and shed two to four months later. Diffuse thinning rather than patchy loss. Drug culprits: anticoagulants (heparin, warfarin), beta-blockers, lithium, retinoids, ACE inhibitors, oral contraceptives upon discontinuation, valproate, and any major physiologic stress.

[pause=1]

Pattern alopecia, the androgenetic baldness pattern in men and women, is not a drug reaction in most cases, though finasteride and minoxidil are the FDA-approved treatments.

[pause=2]

## Nail Assessment

[pause=1]

The nails are quick to inspect and rich in clues.

[pause=1]

Clubbing. The angle between the nail plate and the proximal nail fold approaches or exceeds 180 degrees. The fingertip becomes bulbous. Causes: chronic hypoxic lung disease, cyanotic heart disease, inflammatory bowel disease, certain malignancies. Important: in adults, new clubbing is a red flag worth investigating.

[pause=1]

Koilonychia. Spoon-shaped concave nails. Classic for iron deficiency anemia, but also seen in trauma and hemochromatosis.

[pause=1]

Beau's lines. Transverse depressions across the nail plate. Marks a period of severe illness, chemotherapy, or significant trauma about three months prior, since nails grow roughly one millimeter per month.

[pause=1]

Onycholysis. Separation of the nail from the nail bed. Causes include thyroid disease, psoriasis, fungal infection, and the tetracyclines and fluoroquinolones, especially with photo-onycholysis.

[pause=1]

Half-and-half nails. The proximal half white, the distal half brown or red. Associated with chronic kidney disease.

[pause=1]

Mees' lines. Single or multiple transverse white bands. Classic for arsenic exposure but also seen with thallium poisoning, chemotherapy, and severe systemic illness.

[pause=1]

Splinter hemorrhages. Linear red-brown streaks under the nail. Most commonly trauma. When in the distal nail bed of multiple fingers without trauma, consider infective endocarditis or vasculitis.

[pause=2]

## Pharmacist Pearls

[pause=1]

Pearl one. The single most important question for a new rash on a pharmacy visit: "When did you start any new medication, including over the counter, supplements, and one-time prescriptions?" Patients forget short courses of antibiotics. They forget the friend's old pain pill. Ask broadly.

[pause=1]

Pearl two. Mucosal involvement plus skin involvement plus systemic symptoms equals dangerous until proven otherwise.

[pause=1]

Pearl three. The morbilliform drug rash of the first exposure can be the prodrome of DRESS on the second exposure. Document every drug rash so the next pharmacist or prescriber knows the history.

[pause=1]

Pearl four. Photo-protection counseling at dispensing is part of the pharmacist's job for drugs known to cause phototoxicity, even before any rash appears.

[pause=2]

## Red Flags

[pause=1]

Painful skin. Mucosal erosions in mouth or eyes. Bullae or skin sloughing. Positive Nikolsky. Facial edema with fever. Eosinophilia with rash. Purpura that does not blanch and is spreading. Hypotension or wheezing with hives.

[pause=1]

Any one of these: stop the suspected drug if you can identify it, and refer to emergency care.

[pause=2]

## Audio Quiz

[pause=1]

Five questions.

[pause=2]

### Question one.

A patient three weeks into phenytoin for new-onset seizures presents with fever, facial swelling, a widespread red rash, and an AST that has doubled. What is the diagnosis until proven otherwise?

[pause=5]

The answer: DRESS. Aromatic anticonvulsants are classic culprits. The combination of fever, facial edema, rash, and hepatic involvement at two to eight weeks is the textbook presentation. Stop the drug and refer.

[pause=2]

### Question two.

A patient on a five-day course of trimethoprim-sulfamethoxazole returns on day six with painful red dusky patches on the chest, oral ulcers, and conjunctival injection. What is the lesion type and the action?

[pause=5]

The answer: Stevens-Johnson syndrome. Atypical target lesions with mucosal involvement on a sulfonamide is a dermatologic emergency. Stop the drug immediately and refer to the emergency department.

[pause=2]

### Question three.

A patient on doxycycline reports a sunburn-like rash on her face and arms after a beach day, where she usually doesn't burn this severely. What category of drug reaction?

[pause=5]

The answer: phototoxic reaction. Doxycycline is a classic phototoxic agent. Counsel on sun protection, switch to an alternative when feasible, and reassure that this is not an allergy.

[pause=2]

### Question four.

A patient on chemotherapy three months ago presents with a single horizontal depression across all of her nails. What is the finding and what does it mean?

[pause=5]

The answer: Beau's lines. They mark a period of severe physiologic stress or chemotherapy and grow out at about one millimeter per month, so their position dates the insult.

[pause=2]

### Question five.

A patient on warfarin reports diffuse hair shedding when she brushes her hair, starting about three months ago. What is the most likely mechanism, and what is the management?

[pause=5]

The answer: telogen effluvium induced by warfarin. Hair is shifted to the resting phase by the drug or stress and shed two to four months later. The hair will regrow once the trigger resolves or is changed. Counsel on the expected timeline and discuss alternatives if it's a major concern, but do not stop anticoagulation without discussion if the indication is strong.
