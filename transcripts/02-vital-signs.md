# Episode Two. Vital Signs and the General Survey.

[pause=1]

Welcome back. In episode one we built the foundation: the four exam techniques, the standard sequence, the SOAP note, and clinical reasoning. Today, we make that foundation operational with the assessment most pharmacists perform every day: vital signs and the general survey.

[pause=1]

If you do nothing else well in a pharmacist encounter, you must take vital signs accurately. A wrong blood pressure leads to a wrong therapy decision. A missed orthostatic drop leads to a fall. A normal respiratory rate that wasn't actually measured masks early sepsis. Vital signs are not vital because they're routine. They're vital because they're the most sensitive early-warning system in clinical medicine.

[pause=2]

## Learning Objectives

[pause=1]

By the end of this episode, you should be able to do six things.

One. Take a blood pressure measurement using correct technique and identify the most common technical errors.

Two. Perform orthostatic vital signs and interpret the results.

Three. Measure heart rate and respiratory rate, including counting respirations without the patient noticing.

Four. Interpret pulse oximetry and understand its limitations.

Five. Recognize the four routes of temperature measurement and when each is appropriate.

Six. Conduct a thirty-second general survey that captures critical information the moment the patient walks in.

## Blood Pressure: The Technique That Most People Do Wrong

[pause=1]

Studies consistently show that fewer than one in five blood pressure measurements in routine practice meet guideline-recommended technique. As a pharmacist, you can dramatically improve the data your team makes decisions on by simply doing this right.

[pause=1]

### Patient Preparation

[pause=1]

The patient should be seated quietly for five minutes before the measurement. Feet flat on the floor. Back supported. Legs uncrossed. Arm supported at heart level. Bladder empty. No caffeine, exercise, or smoking in the prior thirty minutes. No talking during the measurement.

[pause=1]

That five-minute rest is the single most violated rule in blood pressure measurement. A patient who walked briskly into clinic and sits down to a cuff thirty seconds later can register ten to twenty millimeters of mercury higher than their true resting pressure.

[pause=2]

### Cuff Size

[pause=1]

A cuff that is too small overestimates blood pressure. A cuff that is too large underestimates it. The bladder length should encircle eighty percent of the arm. The bladder width should be forty percent of the arm circumference.

[pause=1]

In practical terms: a small adult cuff fits arms twenty-two to twenty-six centimeters. An adult cuff fits twenty-seven to thirty-four. A large adult cuff fits thirty-five to forty-four. A thigh cuff is needed beyond that. Always measure the patient's arm circumference, especially when readings seem inconsistent with the clinical picture.

[pause=2]

### Technique

[pause=1]

Center the bladder over the brachial artery. The lower edge of the cuff should sit two to three centimeters above the antecubital fossa. Palpate the radial pulse, inflate to thirty millimeters of mercury above the point where the pulse disappears, then release at two to three millimeters per second. The first Korotkoff sound is systolic. The disappearance of sound, the fifth Korotkoff phase, is diastolic.

[pause=1]

Take two readings, one to two minutes apart, and average them. If they differ by more than five millimeters of mercury, take a third.

[pause=2]

### When to Use Both Arms

[pause=1]

At the first visit, measure blood pressure in both arms. A consistent difference of more than ten to fifteen millimeters of mercury between arms suggests peripheral arterial disease or, rarely, an aortic dissection or coarctation. Once you've identified the higher arm, use that arm for all future readings.

[pause=2]

## Orthostatic Vital Signs

[pause=1]

Orthostatic hypotension is a pharmacist's bread and butter. Many of the drugs we manage cause it: alpha-blockers, diuretics, tricyclic antidepressants, certain antipsychotics, levodopa, nitrates, and high-dose ACE inhibitors and ARBs, especially in volume-depleted patients.

[pause=1]

The definition. A drop in systolic blood pressure of twenty millimeters of mercury or more, or a drop in diastolic of ten or more, within three minutes of standing.

[pause=1]

The technique. Have the patient lie supine for five minutes. Take the blood pressure and heart rate. Have the patient stand. Wait one minute, take blood pressure and heart rate. Wait two more minutes, take blood pressure and heart rate one more time.

[pause=1]

Now interpret. A drop in pressure with an appropriate compensatory rise in heart rate, more than twenty beats per minute, suggests volume depletion. A drop in pressure without a rise in heart rate suggests autonomic dysfunction, which you see in diabetes, Parkinson's disease, and certain beta-blocker patients whose heart rate response is blunted.

[pause=2]

## Heart Rate

[pause=1]

Count the radial pulse for thirty seconds and multiply by two for a regular rhythm. For an irregular rhythm, count for a full sixty seconds, and always auscultate the apical pulse at the cardiac apex with your stethoscope, because peripheral pulses in atrial fibrillation can be missed and you'll underestimate the rate. The difference between the apical and radial pulse is the pulse deficit, a sign of inefficient ejection.

[pause=1]

Normal adult resting heart rate is sixty to one hundred. Tachycardia is over one hundred. Bradycardia is under sixty. Pay attention to medications that drive these: beta-blockers and non-dihydropyridine calcium channel blockers slow the rate. Albuterol, theophylline, and stimulants raise it.

[pause=2]

## Respiratory Rate

[pause=1]

Respiratory rate is the most under-measured vital sign. Studies show clinicians often write down twelve or sixteen as a placeholder without actually counting. Don't do that.

[pause=1]

Here is the trick. Count respirations without telling the patient you are doing so, because conscious patients alter their breathing when they know you are watching. The technique: after taking the radial pulse, continue to hold the wrist as if still counting, and watch the rise and fall of the chest. Count for thirty seconds and multiply by two, or sixty seconds if irregular.

[pause=1]

Normal adult respiratory rate is twelve to twenty. A rate over twenty in an adult is one of the earliest signs of sepsis, anxiety, pain, acidosis, or pulmonary disease. A rate below twelve in a patient on opioids is a red flag for respiratory depression.

[pause=2]

## Pulse Oximetry

[pause=1]

Pulse oximetry measures the percentage of hemoglobin saturated with oxygen, abbreviated SpO2. Normal is ninety-five percent or higher on room air at sea level. Below ninety percent is significant hypoxemia.

[pause=1]

The pearls and pitfalls.

Pearl one. Pulse ox does not detect ventilation. A patient retaining carbon dioxide can have a perfectly normal SpO2 while heading toward respiratory failure. SpO2 is oxygenation, not ventilation.

Pearl two. Carbon monoxide poisoning falsely elevates pulse ox readings, because carboxyhemoglobin absorbs light at the same wavelength as oxyhemoglobin. A patient with a smoke exposure and a normal SpO2 still needs a co-oximetry blood gas.

Pearl three. Cold fingers, nail polish, dark skin pigmentation, and poor perfusion all degrade accuracy. If the waveform is poor, the number is suspect.

Pearl four. The dyshemoglobinemias, like methemoglobinemia from dapsone or topical benzocaine, give an SpO2 that pins around eighty-five percent regardless of true oxygenation. A patient on dapsone with cyanosis and an SpO2 stuck at eighty-five needs a methemoglobin level.

[pause=2]

## Temperature

[pause=1]

Four routes to know. Oral, tympanic, axillary, and rectal. Each has a different normal and a different use case.

[pause=1]

Oral is the workhorse for cooperative adults. Normal is around 36.8 degrees Celsius, 98.2 Fahrenheit. Fever begins at 38.0 Celsius, 100.4 Fahrenheit. Recent hot or cold drinks make it unreliable.

[pause=1]

Tympanic is fast but operator-dependent. The probe must point at the tympanic membrane. Cerumen impaction throws it off. It reads about half a degree Celsius higher than oral.

[pause=1]

Axillary is the least accurate but useful in patients who can't tolerate oral measurement. It reads about half a degree Celsius lower than oral.

[pause=1]

Rectal is the gold standard for core temperature, used in critically ill patients and infants. It reads about half a degree higher than oral. Pharmacists rarely use this route.

[pause=1]

Drug-related thermoregulation problems to remember. Neuroleptic malignant syndrome from antipsychotics: high fever, rigidity, autonomic instability, altered mental status. Serotonin syndrome from serotonergic drug interactions: hyperthermia, clonus, hyperreflexia, agitation. Malignant hyperthermia from inhaled anesthetics and succinylcholine: extreme temperature elevation in the operating room. Anticholinergic toxicity: hot as a hare, dry as a bone, red as a beet, blind as a bat, mad as a hatter.

[pause=2]

## Pain

[pause=1]

Pain is considered the fifth vital sign in many institutions. You assess it on a zero to ten numerical rating scale for adults, the Wong-Baker faces scale for children and patients with limited communication, and the FLACC scale for nonverbal patients.

[pause=1]

But a number is not enough. Use the OLDCARTS mnemonic: Onset, Location, Duration, Character, Aggravating factors, Relieving factors, Timing, and Severity. A patient who says "I have a headache, six out of ten" tells you nothing about whether this is a tension headache, a migraine, or a subarachnoid hemorrhage. The OLDCARTS narrative does.

[pause=2]

## The General Survey

[pause=1]

The general survey is the thirty-second mental snapshot you take as the patient enters the room, walks to the chair, and sits down. This is one of the most powerful, and most overlooked, parts of a pharmacist visit.

[pause=1]

What to capture in thirty seconds.

Apparent age versus stated age. Does the patient look their stated age, or significantly older? Premature aging is a sign of chronic illness, smoking, severe stress.

Level of consciousness and alertness. Are they alert and oriented? Drowsy? Confused?

Skin color and apparent perfusion. Pink, pale, jaundiced, cyanotic, flushed.

Posture and gait. Is the gait steady or shuffling? Are they hunched forward suggesting pain? Are they leaning on the cane on the wrong side?

Speech. Clear and at a normal rate, or slurred, pressured, slow?

Hygiene and self-care. Is the patient groomed appropriately for their context?

Nutritional status. Cachectic, normal, overweight, obese?

Affect and engagement. Flat, anxious, irritable, appropriate?

[pause=1]

A pharmacist who develops the discipline to perform this thirty-second survey at every visit catches more medication problems than any single lab value. A patient who used to walk in briskly and now shuffles. A patient who used to be cheerful and now appears flat. A patient who has lost twenty pounds since their last visit. These are clues.

[pause=2]

## Pharmacist Pearls

[pause=1]

Pearl one. Always re-check an out-of-pattern vital sign. A blood pressure of 80 over 50 in a patient who looks well is more likely a measurement error than impending shock. Re-take it. Different arm if needed.

[pause=1]

Pearl two. Trends matter more than single values. A blood pressure that has crept from 120 over 75 to 138 over 88 over six months on the same regimen is a different patient than one who has been 138 over 88 forever.

[pause=1]

Pearl three. White-coat hypertension is real. If the in-clinic reading is high but the patient's home readings are normal, trust the home readings, especially if the cuff is validated and the technique is correct.

[pause=1]

Pearl four. Masked hypertension is also real and more dangerous. A normal in-clinic reading with elevated home or ambulatory readings carries cardiovascular risk equivalent to sustained hypertension and gets missed all the time.

[pause=2]

## Red Flags

[pause=1]

Blood pressure above 180 over 120 with symptoms — chest pain, vision change, neurologic deficit, severe headache. This is hypertensive emergency. Refer immediately.

Heart rate persistently over 120 at rest, or under 40, with symptoms. Same-day evaluation.

Respiratory rate over 24 in an adult, or under 8, especially on opioids. Same-day evaluation.

SpO2 below 90 percent on room air in a patient who is not in known chronic respiratory failure. Same-day evaluation.

Temperature above 39.5 Celsius, 103 Fahrenheit, with altered mental status. Same-day evaluation.

Orthostatic drop with syncope. Same-day evaluation.

## Audio Quiz

[pause=1]

Five questions. Five seconds each.

[pause=2]

### Question one.

A patient on a thiazide and an ACE inhibitor reports feeling dizzy when standing. Supine blood pressure is 132 over 80, heart rate 72. After one minute standing, blood pressure is 108 over 64, heart rate 95. What is your interpretation?

[pause=5]

The answer: orthostatic hypotension consistent with volume depletion. The systolic dropped 24, exceeding the 20-point threshold, and the heart rate appropriately rose by 23 beats per minute. The likely culprit is the thiazide. Consider reducing or holding the diuretic and checking electrolytes and kidney function.

[pause=2]

### Question two.

A nursing student writes down a respiratory rate of 16 for every patient. Why is this dangerous?

[pause=5]

The answer: respiratory rate is one of the earliest signs of sepsis, opioid toxicity, and pulmonary deterioration. Writing a placeholder value of 16 systematically masks early decompensation and delays recognition of life-threatening conditions.

[pause=2]

### Question three.

A patient on dapsone for pneumocystis prophylaxis presents with mild dyspnea, dusky lips, and an SpO2 of 84 percent that won't budge despite oxygen. What is the likely diagnosis?

[pause=5]

The answer: methemoglobinemia, a known adverse effect of dapsone. The SpO2 pins around 85 percent because methemoglobin absorbs light at the same wavelength as oxyhemoglobin. Confirm with a methemoglobin level, hold the dapsone, and the patient may need methylene blue.

[pause=2]

### Question four.

A blood pressure reading is 156 over 96 in a thin-armed older adult using a standard adult cuff that wraps almost twice around the arm. What is the likely problem?

[pause=5]

The answer: the cuff is too large for this patient. A cuff that is too large underestimates blood pressure. Wait — in this case the cuff is over-large and the reading is high, so the true reading might be even higher. Recheck with a small adult cuff that fits properly.

[pause=2]

### Question five.

A patient on haloperidol presents with a temperature of 40 Celsius, muscle rigidity, altered mental status, and a heart rate of 130. What syndrome must you rule out?

[pause=5]

The answer: neuroleptic malignant syndrome. The combination of antipsychotic exposure, hyperthermia, rigidity, and autonomic instability is the classic tetrad. Stop the antipsychotic and refer for emergency management. Serotonin syndrome looks similar but features clonus and hyperreflexia and follows serotonergic drug exposure.
