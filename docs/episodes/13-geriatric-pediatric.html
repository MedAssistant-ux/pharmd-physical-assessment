Episode Thirteen. Geriatric and Pediatric Variations.

Welcome back. Adults are not all alike. The exam techniques and norms we've covered shift at the extremes of age. Today we'll cover what changes in the geriatric and pediatric patient and the pharmacist-relevant assessments unique to each.

Learning Objectives

One. Apply geriatric assessment tools: frailty, gait and balance, cognition, and polypharmacy review.

Two. Recognize age-related changes that mimic disease.

Three. Identify high-risk medications in older adults.

Four. Use age-appropriate pediatric vital sign ranges.

Five. Understand basic growth chart interpretation and BMI categorization.

Geriatric Assessment

The geriatric population is heterogeneous. A robust 80-year-old who walks a mile a day and a frail 80-year-old in a nursing home are not the same patient. Pharmacotherapy must account for this.

Frailty

Several validated tools. The Fried Frailty Phenotype identifies frailty when three or more of the following are present.

Unintentional weight loss of ten pounds or more in the past year.

Self-reported exhaustion.

Weakness measured by grip strength.

Slow walking speed.

Low physical activity.

The Clinical Frailty Scale is a nine-point scale from "very fit" to "terminally ill" based on global assessment. It's quick and useful at the bedside.

A frail patient needs more conservative pharmacotherapy. Start low. Go slow. Reassess often. Frail patients are at higher risk of falls, delirium, and adverse drug events. Their goal of care is often function and comfort over disease-specific targets.

Falls Assessment

Ask every older adult at every visit: have you fallen in the past year? Are you afraid of falling? Are you unsteady when you stand or walk?

Timed Up and Go test. Have the patient rise from a standard chair without using arms, walk three meters, turn, return, and sit down. Time it. Twelve seconds or more suggests increased fall risk. Fifteen seconds or more is a major red flag.

Orthostatic vital signs as covered in episode two.

Visual acuity, hearing, foot exam, and footwear are all components of the falls assessment.

The high-yield pharmacist intervention. Deprescribe falls-risk medications when possible. The major culprits.

Benzodiazepines and Z-drugs.

Sedating antidepressants and anticholinergics.

First-generation antihistamines.

Antipsychotics.

Opioids.

Antihypertensives, particularly when polypharmacy or orthostasis is present.

Hypoglycemics in patients with frequent hypoglycemia.

Cognition

A baseline assessment with the Mini-Cog at every visit takes ninety seconds. Three-word recall, clock draw, three-word recall again. A failure on either component warrants formal assessment.

Drugs to consider stopping in cognitive impairment. Strong anticholinergics. Benzodiazepines. Tricyclic antidepressants. Skeletal muscle relaxants. Use the Beers Criteria as a reference.

Polypharmacy

A patient on five or more medications is "polypharmaceutical." A patient on ten or more is at very high risk of an adverse drug event. The pharmacist's role.

Medication reconciliation at every transition.

Identify potentially inappropriate medications using Beers Criteria or STOPP/START criteria.

Identify prescribing cascades, where a side effect of one drug is treated with another drug. The classic example: a calcium channel blocker causes ankle edema, the patient is given a diuretic, the diuretic causes hyperuricemia, the patient is given allopurinol.

Anticholinergic burden score. Many drugs that older adults take have anticholinergic activity, including diphenhydramine, oxybutynin, paroxetine, amitriptyline, scopolamine, certain bladder agents, and many more. Cumulative burden raises delirium risk.

Age-Related Changes That Mimic Disease

Skin: thinner, drier, with senile purpura on the forearms from minor trauma. Not a coagulopathy.

Pupils: smaller, react more sluggishly.

Hearing: presbycusis, high-frequency hearing loss.

Posture: kyphosis from osteoporotic vertebral compression.

Reflexes: ankle reflex may be diminished bilaterally without pathology.

Vital signs: resting heart rate slightly lower, systolic blood pressure rises, pulse pressure widens from arterial stiffening.

Cognition: slower processing speed is normal; loss of recent memory is not.

Pediatric Assessment

Pediatric pharmacy is its own world. The pharmacist who knows the normal ranges and developmental milestones catches medication problems other clinicians miss.

Pediatric Vital Sign Ranges

Heart rate (awake, beats per minute, approximate normal ranges).

Newborn (zero to three months): 100 to 160.

Infant (three to twelve months): 90 to 150.

Toddler (one to three years): 80 to 140.

Preschool (three to five): 80 to 120.

School-age (five to twelve): 70 to 110.

Adolescent (twelve to eighteen): 60 to 100.

Respiratory rate (breaths per minute).

Newborn: 30 to 60.

Infant: 24 to 38.

Toddler: 22 to 30.

Preschool: 20 to 24.

School-age: 18 to 24.

Adolescent: 12 to 20.

Blood pressure: rough normal systolic in mmHg.

Newborn: 60 to 80.

Infant: 70 to 95.

Toddler: 80 to 100.

Preschool: 90 to 105.

School-age: 95 to 110.

Adolescent: 100 to 130.

Use percentile-based normograms in clinic for accurate interpretation by sex, age, and height.

A child whose heart rate or respiratory rate is at the high end of normal in a stressful setting may be reasonable. A child whose vital signs are above the maximum at rest is a problem until proven otherwise.

Growth Charts and BMI

Plot length or height, weight, head circumference (under age three), and BMI (age two and up) on age- and sex-specific CDC or WHO charts. Track over time. A child should track along a percentile curve.

Failure to thrive. A drop across two major percentile lines is concerning. Causes range from caloric insufficiency to underlying disease, including chronic medications such as ADHD stimulants suppressing appetite.

Pediatric BMI categories use percentiles, not adult cutoffs. Underweight: less than 5th percentile. Healthy weight: 5th to less than 85th. Overweight: 85th to less than 95th. Obesity: 95th or higher. Severe obesity: 120 percent of the 95th percentile.

Developmental Considerations

A few high-yield milestones the pharmacist may encounter.

Two months: smiles socially.

Four months: holds head up steady, coos.

Six months: rolls over both ways, sits with support, transfers objects.

Nine months: sits without support, says "mama" and "dada" non-specifically.

Twelve months: pulls to stand, may take first steps, uses "mama" and "dada" with meaning, follows simple commands.

Eighteen months: walks well, uses ten to twenty words.

Two years: combines two words, runs.

A child not meeting key milestones warrants referral. Some medications, like certain anticonvulsants, can affect development; teratogenic exposures in utero matter for life.

Pediatric Physical Exam Modifications

Conduct as much of the exam as possible while the child is calm, often in the parent's lap. Save the unpleasant parts, throat exam, otoscopy, abdominal palpation, for the end.

Inspect the fontanelles in infants under eighteen months. A bulging fontanelle suggests elevated intracranial pressure. A sunken fontanelle suggests dehydration.

Capillary refill in children is a critical sign of perfusion. Press on the sternum or a fingernail for five seconds, release. Refill should be under two seconds. Greater than three seconds is delayed.

Skin turgor for hydration assessment in infants and toddlers.

For pediatric vital signs, count respiratory rate when the child is asleep or quietly resting; crying invalidates the count.

Drugs With Special Considerations in Children

Aspirin: avoid in viral illness due to Reye's syndrome risk.

Tetracyclines: avoid in children under eight (permanent tooth discoloration).

Fluoroquinolones: caution in growing children (potential cartilage effects), though use is increasing in selected indications.

Codeine: avoided in children due to variable CYP2D6 metabolism causing unpredictable opioid effect.

Promethazine: avoid in children under two (respiratory depression risk).

Acetaminophen and ibuprofen: appropriate weight-based dosing prevents toxic ingestions. Counsel parents always.

Pharmacist Pearls

Pearl one. "Start low, go slow" is the geriatric mantra. So is "start low, go slow, but go" — undertreatment is also an error.

Pearl two. Every geriatric visit should screen for falls, polypharmacy, cognition, and goals of care.

Pearl three. Pediatric doses are weight-based. Calculate, double-check, and counsel parents on the exact volume and dosing device. Avoid teaspoons; insist on a milliliter syringe.

Pearl four. Question new prescriptions written to treat a side effect of an existing drug. The right answer is often to deprescribe rather than to add.

Red Flags

Geriatric: new functional decline, new confusion, recent fall, unintentional weight loss, multiple medications without indication review for over a year.

Pediatric: bulging fontanelle, delayed capillary refill, lethargy, paradoxical irritability, dehydration, failure to thrive, missed major milestones.

Audio Quiz

Five questions.

Question one.

An 82-year-old with mild cognitive impairment is on diphenhydramine for sleep, oxybutynin for bladder, and paroxetine for depression. What concept describes this medication list, and what is your action?

The answer: high anticholinergic burden. Deprescribe. Replace diphenhydramine with non-pharmacologic sleep strategies or melatonin. Switch oxybutynin to mirabegron. Switch paroxetine to a less anticholinergic SSRI like sertraline or escitalopram, or to an alternative class.

Question two.

A 2-year-old has had three episodes of acute otitis media in six months. The parent asks about prophylactic aspirin during the next viral illness. What is your response?

The answer: do not give aspirin to children during viral illness due to the risk of Reye's syndrome, an acute encephalopathy with hepatic failure. Acetaminophen or ibuprofen at appropriate weight-based doses are appropriate.

Question three.

An 85-year-old patient on amlodipine develops ankle edema. The previous provider added furosemide, then the patient developed hyperuricemia and gout, and was started on allopurinol. What is this pattern called and what is the alternative approach?

The answer: prescribing cascade. The original CCB caused the edema; treating with a diuretic created a second problem; treating that created a third. Better approach: switch the CCB or add an ACE inhibitor to counter the edema, and deprescribe the diuretic and allopurinol if appropriate.

Question four.

An infant has a sunken anterior fontanelle, dry mucous membranes, decreased urine output, and capillary refill of three seconds. What is the clinical picture and the priority?

The answer: moderate to severe dehydration. Priority is fluid resuscitation, oral rehydration if tolerated, or IV access if not. Identify and treat the underlying cause and recheck within hours.

Question five.

An 80-year-old patient with frailty is referred for tight blood pressure control to a target of 120 systolic. What considerations should inform the therapy plan?

The answer: frail older adults benefit from less aggressive blood pressure targets to avoid orthostatic hypotension, falls, and renal injury. Consider a target around 130 to 140 systolic with attention to standing pressures and falls history. The goal is functional improvement and event prevention, not chasing a number that the patient cannot tolerate.
