Episode Eight. The Abdominal Exam.

Welcome back. The abdomen is the one body region that breaks the standard exam sequence. The order is inspect, auscultate, percuss, palpate. Why? Because pressing on the abdomen alters bowel sounds, and you want to hear the abdomen the way the body left it. Master the sequence, learn the regions, and know the drug-induced abdominal findings, and you will perform a clinically useful exam at every visit.

Learning Objectives

One. Describe the abdominal regions and the organs underlying each.

Two. Inspect the abdomen for contour, scars, and pulsations.

Three. Auscultate bowel sounds and vascular sounds.

Four. Percuss liver and spleen size and identify shifting dullness of ascites.

Five. Palpate light, deep, and for the liver edge.

Six. Recognize peritoneal signs and the differential they support.

Seven. Identify common drug-related GI findings.

Mapping the Abdomen

Two conventions exist. The four-quadrant system divides the abdomen with a vertical line through the umbilicus and a horizontal line through the umbilicus, giving you right upper quadrant, left upper quadrant, right lower quadrant, left lower quadrant. The nine-region system divides further, into epigastric, umbilical, hypogastric in the midline; and right and left hypochondriac, lumbar, and iliac regions.

Right upper quadrant: liver, gallbladder, right kidney, pylorus, duodenum, head of the pancreas, hepatic flexure of the colon.

Left upper quadrant: stomach, spleen, left kidney, body and tail of the pancreas, splenic flexure of the colon.

Right lower quadrant: cecum, appendix, ascending colon, right ovary and fallopian tube in women, ureter.

Left lower quadrant: descending and sigmoid colon, left ovary and fallopian tube, ureter.

Epigastric: stomach, duodenum, pancreas, aorta.

Hypogastric: bladder, uterus, sigmoid colon.

Periumbilical: small bowel, transverse colon, mesentery.

The pearl. Pain location does not equal anatomic source. Periumbilical pain may originate from the appendix in early appendicitis. Right shoulder pain may originate from the diaphragm or the gallbladder, referred via the phrenic nerve.

Inspection

Have the patient supine with the abdomen exposed from below the breasts to the pubic symphysis. Stand on the right side.

What to inspect.

Contour: flat, rounded, scaphoid (sunken), or protuberant. Distention can be from fat, fluid, flatus, fetus, feces, or fatal masses — the "six Fs."

Symmetry: a focal bulge suggests a hernia or mass.

Surface findings: scars (note location, suggesting prior surgery), striae (silvery in old pregnancy or weight loss; purple in Cushing's syndrome from corticosteroids or endogenous excess), spider angiomata above the umbilicus (suggest chronic liver disease, especially in alcoholic or viral hepatitis patients), caput medusae (engorged periumbilical veins of portal hypertension), Cullen's sign (periumbilical bluish discoloration of retroperitoneal hemorrhage), Grey-Turner's sign (flank bluish discoloration of pancreatic hemorrhage).

Pulsations: a visible aortic pulsation in a thin patient is normal. A pulsatile, expansile mass is concerning for an abdominal aortic aneurysm.

Peristalsis: visible waves are abnormal in adults and suggest bowel obstruction.

Auscultation

Warm the diaphragm. Place it gently on the abdomen. Listen for at least one minute in one quadrant if no sounds are heard, before concluding sounds are absent.

Bowel sounds: normal frequency is five to thirty per minute, irregular gurgling.

Hyperactive bowel sounds: increased motility, as in early bowel obstruction, gastroenteritis, GI bleeding.

Hypoactive or absent bowel sounds: ileus, peritonitis, late bowel obstruction. Postoperative ileus is the most common cause in inpatients.

High-pitched tinkling sounds: small bowel obstruction.

Vascular sounds: listen over the abdominal aorta in the midline, then over the renal arteries to either side of the midline, the iliac arteries in the lower abdomen, and the femoral arteries in the groin. A bruit suggests turbulent flow from atherosclerotic stenosis or aneurysm.

Friction rubs: rare, over the liver in hepatic abscess or tumor; over the spleen in infarct.

Percussion

Percuss systematically in all four quadrants. Most of the abdomen should be tympanic from gas-filled bowel, with dullness over solid organs.

Liver Span

Percuss downward in the right midclavicular line from chest resonance into liver dullness. Mark the upper border. Percuss upward from below the umbilicus into liver dullness. Mark the lower border. Measure between marks.

Normal liver span at the midclavicular line is six to twelve centimeters. Greater than twelve suggests hepatomegaly. Hepatomegaly causes: heart failure, infiltrative diseases, hepatitis, cirrhosis (though end-stage cirrhotic livers may shrink), metastatic disease, drug-induced hepatic injury.

Splenic Percussion

Percuss the lowest left intercostal space in the anterior axillary line, the "Traube's space." Normally tympanic. Dullness suggests splenomegaly.

Ascites

Two findings.

Shifting dullness: with the patient supine, mark the boundary between tympany and dullness on each side. Have the patient roll onto one side. After thirty seconds, percuss again. If the boundary has shifted because fluid has moved, ascites is suggested.

Fluid wave: with the patient supine and an assistant pressing the ulnar edges of both hands along the midline to block fat-wave transmission, tap one flank firmly. A wave palpable on the opposite flank suggests ascites.

Pharmacist relevance: ascites in a chronic alcohol user with spider angiomata and palmar erythema points to cirrhosis. Drug doses must be adjusted. Avoid NSAIDs, which can precipitate hepatorenal syndrome. Use caution with all hepatically cleared drugs.

Palpation

Start with light palpation in all four quadrants, then deep palpation. Always start away from the area the patient identifies as painful.

Light palpation: about one centimeter deep. Assess for tenderness, guarding, and superficial masses.

Deep palpation: four to five centimeters deep. Assess for organ enlargement, deeper masses, and aortic pulsation.

Guarding

Voluntary guarding: tension that the patient consciously creates in anticipation of pain. Relaxes with distraction or deep breaths.

Involuntary guarding, also called rigidity: persistent reflex contraction of the abdominal wall that does not relax. Indicates peritoneal inflammation, a surgical emergency.

Peritoneal Signs

Rebound tenderness: press deeply, then quickly release. Pain on release suggests peritoneal irritation. Use sparingly — the test is uncomfortable and not specific.

Cough test: a patient with peritonitis cannot bear to cough without exacerbated pain.

Heel-strike test: have the patient stand on their toes and drop down on the heels. Pain suggests peritonitis.

Specific Signs

Murphy's sign: with deep palpation under the right costal margin, the patient inhales. If the patient stops mid-inhalation due to sudden right upper quadrant pain, suggesting acute cholecystitis.

McBurney's point tenderness: pain about two-thirds of the way from the umbilicus to the right anterior superior iliac spine — classic for appendicitis.

Rovsing's sign: palpation of the left lower quadrant elicits right lower quadrant pain. Suggests appendicitis.

Psoas sign: extension of the right hip elicits pain. Inflamed retrocecal appendix or psoas abscess.

Obturator sign: passive flexion and internal rotation of the right hip elicits pain. Pelvic appendix or pelvic abscess.

Cullen's and Grey-Turner's signs: as noted in inspection — late signs of pancreatic hemorrhage.

Organ Palpation

Liver edge: place one hand below the right costal margin, the other under the right flank for support. Ask the patient to take a deep breath. The descending liver edge may meet your fingers. A normal liver edge is smooth, soft, and only slightly palpable below the costal margin in many patients. A firm, nodular, or markedly enlarged edge is pathologic.

Spleen: from the right side, reach across with one hand under the left flank for support, palpate just below the left costal margin with the other. Ask the patient to take a deep breath. A normally-sized spleen is not palpable. A palpable spleen at the costal margin is enlarged; splenic enlargement can result from infection (EBV, malaria), hematologic disease (leukemia, lymphoma), congestive states (portal hypertension), and others.

Kidneys: rarely palpable in normal adults. May feel the lower pole of the right kidney in thin patients. Punch the costovertebral angle on each side to check for tenderness — a positive CVA tenderness suggests pyelonephritis or renal stone.

Drug-Related Abdominal Findings

NSAIDs: gastritis, peptic ulcer, gastrointestinal bleeding manifest as epigastric tenderness, hematemesis, melena. Acute kidney injury can present with CVA tenderness.

Opioids: constipation, decreased bowel sounds, ileus.

Antibiotics, especially clindamycin and fluoroquinolones: C. difficile colitis, presenting with watery diarrhea, lower abdominal pain, sometimes fever.

GLP-1 agonists: delayed gastric emptying, nausea, vomiting, occasional pancreatitis with severe epigastric pain radiating to the back.

Bisphosphonates, oral: esophagitis with retrosternal pain.

Iron supplements: dark stools, constipation, sometimes epigastric pain.

Anticoagulants: GI bleeding, presenting with melena, hematochezia, or abdominal pain from spontaneous retroperitoneal hematoma in unstable patients.

Loperamide and other antidiarrheals: can mask C. difficile and worsen toxic megacolon. Avoid in infectious diarrhea with fever or bloody stool.

Statins: rare hepatotoxicity, presenting with right upper quadrant discomfort and elevated transaminases.

Acetaminophen: hepatotoxicity in overdose, presenting at twenty-four to seventy-two hours with right upper quadrant pain.

Methotrexate, isoniazid, antifungals (azoles), amiodarone, valproate: hepatic injury patterns vary; periodic LFT monitoring required.

Pharmacist Pearls

Pearl one. Auscultate before palpation. It's the one exception to the standard sequence and exam graders test it routinely.

Pearl two. Murphy's sign in a patient on octreotide or a fibrate, both of which increase gallstone risk, raises suspicion for acute cholecystitis.

Pearl three. A patient on chronic NSAIDs with new black stools needs a workup. Don't dismiss it as iron supplements or dietary causes.

Pearl four. New diarrhea after recent antibiotic exposure — within eight weeks — is C. difficile until proven otherwise. Avoid loperamide.

Red Flags

Acute severe abdominal pain. Rigid abdomen with rebound and guarding. Pulsatile abdominal mass over five centimeters: AAA. Bloody emesis or melena with hemodynamic instability. Acute right upper quadrant pain with fever and jaundice: Charcot's triad for ascending cholangitis. Lower abdominal pain with rebound in a woman of childbearing age: ectopic pregnancy must be ruled out.

Audio Quiz

Five questions.

Question one.

In what order do you examine the abdomen, and why?

The answer: inspect, auscultate, percuss, palpate. Auscultation before manual manipulation preserves the unaltered baseline bowel sounds.

Question two.

A patient on chronic prednisone for rheumatoid arthritis presents with purple striae across the abdomen and central obesity. What is the term and what is going on?

The answer: cushingoid features from chronic corticosteroid therapy. Purple striae form because the skin thins and ruptures under tension. This patient is at risk for osteoporosis, hyperglycemia, hypertension, and adrenal suppression. Plan slow tapering and bone protection.

Question three.

A patient on clindamycin for ten days returns with three days of watery diarrhea, mild lower abdominal cramping, and a low-grade fever. What is the most likely diagnosis and what should you avoid recommending?

The answer: C. difficile colitis. Avoid loperamide and other antimotility agents because slowing transit can worsen toxin-mediated injury and precipitate toxic megacolon. Obtain stool testing, stop the offending antibiotic if possible, and treat as indicated.

Question four.

A patient with cirrhosis and ascites has new fever and abdominal pain. What diagnosis must you consider, and what next step is appropriate?

The answer: spontaneous bacterial peritonitis. Diagnostic paracentesis with cell count and culture is the next step. Empiric antibiotics, typically a third-generation cephalosporin, are started if SBP is suspected.

Question five.

A patient on a semaglutide presents with severe epigastric pain radiating to the back, nausea, and vomiting. What complication must be ruled out, and what is your action?

The answer: acute pancreatitis. Stop the semaglutide, refer for evaluation including lipase and imaging, and supportive care. GLP-1 receptor agonists carry a small but real pancreatitis risk that has been recognized since the class was introduced.
