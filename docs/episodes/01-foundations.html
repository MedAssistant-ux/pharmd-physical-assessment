Why Pharmacists Do Physical Assessment

For most of pharmacy's history, the physical exam belonged to physicians and nurses. That world is gone. Today, pharmacists in ambulatory care clinics, transitional care, anticoagulation services, diabetes management, and collaborative practice agreements regularly perform focused physical assessments. You may check blood pressure, listen to lung sounds during an asthma visit, palpate an injection site, examine a diabetic foot, or screen for orthostatic hypotension in a frail older adult.

The key word is focused. You are not performing a complete head-to-toe physical the way a hospitalist would on admission. You are performing a targeted assessment driven by the medication-related problem in front of you. A patient on a statin who reports muscle pain? You're examining strength and tenderness in the proximal muscle groups. A patient on amiodarone? You're inspecting the skin, palpating the thyroid, and asking about visual changes. Your assessment is always tied back to the drug.

Three core skills define competent pharmacist assessment. The first is the ability to take a focused medication-centered history. The second is the ability to perform the relevant physical exam maneuvers correctly. The third is the ability to interpret your findings in the context of the patient's medication regimen and decide what to do next, whether that is adjust, refer, monitor, or reassure.

Scope of Practice

Scope of practice varies by state, by institution, and by your specific collaborative practice agreement. A few principles hold almost universally.

Pharmacists generally can: inspect any external area the patient consents to expose, take vital signs, perform point of care tests under appropriate orders, palpate accessible structures like lymph nodes and the thyroid, and auscultate the heart and lungs when indicated.

Pharmacists generally should not, without specific authorization: perform invasive maneuvers, perform pelvic, breast, rectal, or genital examinations, or document a diagnosis outside their authorized scope.

When in doubt, the rule is simple. Stay inside your collaborative practice agreement. If a finding is outside your scope, document it, communicate it, and refer.

The Four Core Techniques

Every physical exam, regardless of body system, uses some combination of four techniques: inspection, palpation, percussion, and auscultation.

Inspection

Inspection is looking. It is also the most underrated skill in physical assessment. A new student rushes to the stethoscope. An experienced clinician spends thirty seconds simply observing.

When you inspect, you are looking for symmetry, color, shape, movement, and anything that doesn't belong. A subtle facial droop. Uneven chest expansion. Pallor of the conjunctivae. The yellow tint of jaundice. A rash in a drug-eruption distribution. Inspection begins the moment you walk into the room, before you've said hello.

Palpation

Palpation is touching with purpose. You use the pads of your fingers, not the tips, for fine discrimination. You use the dorsum, the back of your hand, for temperature. You use the ulnar surface, the pinky-side of your hand, for vibration. And you use the flat of your palm for broad areas.

Light palpation goes about one centimeter deep, used for tenderness and superficial masses. Deep palpation goes four to five centimeters, used for organs and deeper masses. Always start light, and always start away from the area the patient says hurts. If the patient is guarding their right lower quadrant, you palpate the left upper quadrant first, work your way around, and end in the area of pain.

Percussion

Percussion is tapping a body surface to elicit a sound that tells you what is underneath. Pharmacists use percussion less often than physicians, but you should still recognize the basics.

The technique: place the middle finger of your non-dominant hand flat against the body surface. Tap the distal joint of that finger sharply with the tip of the middle finger of your dominant hand. The wrist does the work, not the elbow.

Five percussion sounds, from softest to loudest. Flat: over solid structures like bone or muscle. Dull: over solid organs like the liver or a consolidated lung. Resonant: the normal sound over healthy lung tissue. Hyperresonant: over hyperinflated lungs as in emphysema or pneumothorax. Tympanic: a drum-like sound over a gas-filled stomach or bowel.

Auscultation

Auscultation is listening with the stethoscope. Two parts to know: the diaphragm and the bell. The diaphragm is the flat side. It is best for high-pitched sounds: normal heart sounds S1 and S2, breath sounds, and bowel sounds. The bell is the cup-shaped side. It is best for low-pitched sounds: S3, S4, and certain murmurs like mitral stenosis.

A practical tip. The stethoscope is not magic. If you put a cold diaphragm on a cold patient, you will hear nothing useful because the patient will be tense and shivering. Warm the diaphragm in your palm for a few seconds first. Press firmly with the diaphragm. Rest the bell lightly so the patient's skin acts as the diaphragm.

The Standard Sequence and the One Exception

For every body system, the standard sequence is: inspect, palpate, percuss, auscultate.

There is one important exception. The abdomen. For the abdomen, the sequence is: inspect, then auscultate, then percuss, then palpate. Why? Because if you press on the abdomen first, you stimulate the bowel and change the bowel sounds you would have heard. So you listen before you touch.

Memorize that. Everywhere else: inspect, palpate, percuss, auscultate. Abdomen: inspect, auscultate, percuss, palpate.

The SOAP Note

Every assessment you perform must be documented, and the universal documentation framework is SOAP. S, O, A, P.

S is for Subjective. What the patient tells you. The chief complaint, the history of present illness, the review of systems. Subjective data are the patient's words and reported symptoms. Pain is subjective. Nausea is subjective. The phrase "I feel dizzy when I stand up" is subjective.

O is for Objective. What you observe and measure. Vital signs are objective. Physical exam findings are objective. Lab values, point of care test results, and the medication list, when verified, are objective. The blood pressure reading of 142 over 88 is objective. The presence of bilateral lower-extremity edema, two-plus pitting, is objective.

A is for Assessment. Your clinical reasoning. This is where you synthesize the subjective and objective data into a problem list. For each problem, you state what it is, whether it is controlled, and what is driving it. A typical pharmacist assessment line might read: "Hypertension, uncontrolled. Average home blood pressure 145 over 92, above goal of less than 130 over 80. Likely contributors: suboptimal lisinopril dose, recent addition of ibuprofen for knee pain."

P is for Plan. What you will do about each problem. The plan should be specific, measurable, and time-bound. Not "increase blood pressure med." Instead: "Increase lisinopril from 10 milligrams daily to 20 milligrams daily. Counsel on home BP monitoring twice daily for two weeks. Follow up in two weeks. Discontinue ibuprofen, switch to scheduled acetaminophen."

Clinical Reasoning: From Complaint to Plan

Clinical reasoning is the bridge between data and decision. Let me give you a framework you can apply to any case.

Step one. Identify the chief complaint or the medication-related problem. What brought the patient in? What flagged this visit?

Step two. Gather a focused history. What makes the symptom better or worse? When did it start? What medications was the patient on when it started?

Step three. Perform the focused exam. What body systems are implicated? What signs would confirm or refute your leading hypotheses?

Step four. Integrate the data. Subjective plus objective. Does the picture fit a medication adverse effect? An untreated condition? A new diagnosis?

Step five. Decide. Adjust the regimen, monitor more closely, refer, or reassure. Document the reasoning in the SOAP note.

The discipline of this framework is what separates a thoughtful pharmacist clinician from someone who just checks boxes. The framework forces you to ask: does my plan actually address the data I gathered?

Pharmacist Pearls

Three pearls to carry into every encounter.

Pearl one. Always tie the physical exam back to a drug. If you cannot articulate why you are performing a specific exam maneuver in terms of a medication-related problem, you are probably not doing the right exam.

Pearl two. Document negative findings that matter. "No peripheral edema. No conjunctival pallor. Cranial nerves two through twelve grossly intact." Negative findings are evidence. They are how you exclude differentials. A blank chart looks like you didn't check.

Pearl three. The patient is watching you. Wash your hands. Introduce yourself. Explain what you are about to do before you do it. Ask permission to touch. Drape appropriately. These small habits are the foundation of clinical professionalism.

Red Flags: When to Stop and Refer

At every encounter, scan for red flags that demand same-day evaluation rather than a pharmacy visit.

Chest pain with diaphoresis, dyspnea, or radiation. Stop and refer.

Acute focal neurologic deficit. New facial droop, slurred speech, unilateral weakness. Stop and refer. This is a stroke until proven otherwise.

Severe headache that is the worst of the patient's life, especially if abrupt onset. Stop and refer.

Signs of severe drug reaction. Confluent rash, mucosal involvement, fever, sloughing skin. This could be Stevens-Johnson syndrome or toxic epidermal necrolysis. Stop, do not give another dose of the suspect drug, and refer.

Suspected sepsis. Fever, tachycardia, hypotension, altered mental status. Stop and refer.

Your job is not to treat these emergencies. Your job is to recognize them and route the patient appropriately.

Audio Quiz

Time for the quiz. Five questions. I'll read the question, give you five seconds to think, and then read the answer with a short explanation.

Question one.

In what order do you examine the abdomen?

The answer: inspect, auscultate, percuss, palpate. The abdomen is the one exception to the standard sequence because palpation first would alter bowel sounds.

Question two.

Which part of the stethoscope is best for low-pitched heart sounds like S3 and S4?

The answer: the bell. The bell, applied lightly, is for low-pitched sounds. The diaphragm, pressed firmly, is for high-pitched sounds like S1, S2, and breath sounds.

Question three.

A patient has a blood pressure reading of 152 over 94 in clinic. Subjective or objective?

The answer: objective. Any measurement you make is objective data. What the patient tells you about their symptoms is subjective.

Question four.

Your patient on a statin reports new bilateral thigh and shoulder pain. Which exam techniques and findings would be most relevant?

The answer: inspection for symmetry and any swelling, palpation of the proximal muscle groups for tenderness, and assessment of strength against resistance. Statin-associated muscle injury classically affects the proximal muscles, the thighs and shoulders, and a tender, weak patient warrants checking a creatine kinase and re-evaluating the statin.

Question five.

A patient on a new sulfonamide antibiotic returns with a fever, a spreading rash, and painful sores in her mouth. What is your immediate action?

The answer: stop the suspected drug and refer for emergency evaluation. This is a red flag for Stevens-Johnson syndrome or toxic epidermal necrolysis. Mucosal involvement plus skin involvement plus systemic symptoms is a dermatologic emergency.
