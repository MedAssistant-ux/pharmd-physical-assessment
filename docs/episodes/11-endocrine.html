Episode Eleven. Endocrine Physical Findings.

Welcome back. Endocrine disease shows itself on the body. The thyroid, the adrenal cortex, the pituitary, the pancreas — all of them have characteristic physical signatures. The pharmacist who can recognize these signatures catches medication adverse effects long before lab abnormalities appear.

Learning Objectives

One. Inspect and palpate the thyroid for size, nodularity, and tenderness.

Two. Recognize the constellations of hyperthyroidism and hypothyroidism.

Three. Perform a diabetic foot exam.

Four. Identify the features of Cushing's syndrome and adrenal insufficiency.

Five. Recognize drug-induced endocrine syndromes.

The Thyroid in Depth

We covered the basics in HEENT. Here we dig in.

Inspection. Stand in front of the patient, extend their neck slightly, give them a sip of water. The thyroid moves up with the swallow. A visible enlarged lobe or symmetric enlargement is a goiter.

Palpation. From behind the patient is often more comfortable. Find the cricoid cartilage as the landmark. The isthmus sits just below it. Each lobe extends laterally and slightly downward. With the patient swallowing, palpate each lobe between your fingers and the trachea.

Describe size as small, normal, mildly enlarged, or markedly enlarged. Consistency: soft, firm, rubbery, or hard. Surface: smooth, nodular, or single nodule. Tenderness, bruit on auscultation.

Hyperthyroidism

Constellation of findings.

Skin: warm, moist, smooth. Pretibial myxedema (a paradoxical name for what is actually a sign of Graves' disease) in some cases.

Hair: fine, soft, may shed.

Eyes: lid lag (the upper lid lags behind as the eye tracks downward), exophthalmos in Graves' disease.

Neck: diffusely enlarged thyroid with bruit (Graves'); single nodule (toxic adenoma); multinodular (toxic multinodular goiter); tender (subacute thyroiditis).

Cardiovascular: tachycardia, atrial fibrillation, widened pulse pressure, hyperdynamic precordium.

Neurologic: fine action tremor, brisk reflexes, anxiety, insomnia.

Musculoskeletal: proximal muscle weakness in severe cases.

Weight: weight loss despite increased appetite.

GI: more frequent stools, sometimes diarrhea.

Thermoregulation: heat intolerance.

Drug-induced hyperthyroidism. Amiodarone (type 1 iodine-load and type 2 destructive thyroiditis). Lithium can cause hyperthyroidism less commonly than hypothyroidism. Immune checkpoint inhibitors and interferon. Exogenous levothyroxine overuse.

Hypothyroidism

Constellation of findings.

Skin: dry, coarse, sometimes yellow-tinged (carotenemia). Decreased sweating.

Hair: coarse, brittle. Loss of lateral third of the eyebrows (queen Anne's sign).

Eyes: periorbital edema.

Neck: enlarged thyroid in Hashimoto's; atrophic and not palpable in late-stage disease.

Cardiovascular: bradycardia, diastolic hypertension, narrow pulse pressure. Pericardial effusion in severe cases.

Neurologic: slow speech, delayed relaxation phase of deep tendon reflexes (the "hung-up" reflex), depression, cognitive slowing.

Musculoskeletal: muscle aches, prolonged DTR relaxation, sometimes myopathy.

Weight: weight gain.

GI: constipation.

Thermoregulation: cold intolerance.

Voice: hoarse, deep.

Carpal tunnel syndrome from tissue infiltration.

Myxedema coma in severe untreated cases: profound bradycardia, hypothermia, hyponatremia, mental status change. Mortality is high.

Drug-induced hypothyroidism. Amiodarone (more often than hyperthyroidism). Lithium. Tyrosine kinase inhibitors like sunitinib. Interferon. Immune checkpoint inhibitors. Iodinated contrast in susceptible patients.

The Diabetic Foot Exam

Every patient with diabetes should have an annual comprehensive foot exam. The pharmacist in a diabetes clinic does this exam.

Inspection. Look at the dorsum, sole, between the toes, around the nails. Inspect for ulcers, calluses, deformities (Charcot foot, hammer toes, hallux valgus), skin breakdown, fungal infections. Check the shoes the patient wore in. Worn unevenly? Too tight?

Pulses. Dorsalis pedis and posterior tibial bilaterally. Absent pulses suggest PAD; check ABI.

Sensation. Use a 10-gram monofilament. Apply at multiple plantar surface sites (typically the great toe, third, and fifth metatarsal heads, plus a few other locations) with enough pressure to buckle the monofilament slightly. The patient looks away and reports feeling each touch. Inability to feel the monofilament at any site is a sign of protective sensation loss and risk of foot ulcer.

Vibration sense with a 128 Hz tuning fork on the great toe.

Achilles reflexes: bilaterally diminished in advanced diabetic neuropathy.

Proprioception of the great toe.

Education at every exam. Daily foot inspection by the patient. Properly fitted shoes. Never go barefoot. Trim nails straight across. Report any new sore immediately. A small unnoticed lesion in a patient with sensory neuropathy can become limb-threatening within days.

Cushing's Syndrome

Endogenous Cushing's syndrome is uncommon, but iatrogenic Cushing's from corticosteroid therapy is everywhere.

Features.

Central obesity with thin extremities.

Moon facies.

Buffalo hump, the dorsocervical fat pad.

Supraclavicular fat pads.

Purple striae on the abdomen, thighs, and breasts, wider than one centimeter.

Easy bruising and thin skin.

Proximal muscle weakness — patient cannot rise from a low chair without using arms.

Hypertension.

Hirsutism and acne in women.

Hyperglycemia.

Osteoporosis.

Mood changes, insomnia, sometimes psychosis.

Pharmacist relevance. Counsel patients on chronic steroids about minimum effective dose, tapering protocols, calcium and vitamin D, bone density screening, infection vigilance, hyperglycemia monitoring, and mood changes. Never abruptly stop chronic steroid therapy; adrenal crisis can follow.

Adrenal Insufficiency

Two patterns.

Primary adrenal insufficiency: Addison's disease. Both glucocorticoid and mineralocorticoid loss. Hyperpigmentation of skin and mucous membranes from elevated ACTH driving melanocyte stimulation. Hypotension, hyperkalemia, hyponatremia, salt craving.

Secondary adrenal insufficiency: pituitary or hypothalamic disease, or, most commonly in pharmacy, suppression from chronic exogenous corticosteroid. Glucocorticoid loss only. No hyperpigmentation, no hyperkalemia. Hypotension and hyponatremia possible.

Adrenal crisis: acute decompensation with severe hypotension, hypoglycemia, mental status change, often precipitated by stress in a patient with chronic insufficiency or suddenly stopped steroids. Treat with intravenous hydrocortisone immediately and refer.

Pharmacist relevance. Stress-dose steroids during major illness or surgery for patients on chronic steroids over 5 mg of prednisone daily for more than three weeks. Patients should carry an emergency card or wear a medical alert bracelet.

Drug-Induced Endocrine Syndromes

Antipsychotic-induced hyperprolactinemia. Galactorrhea, amenorrhea or oligomenorrhea, gynecomastia, sexual dysfunction. Risperidone, paliperidone, haloperidol, and other first-generation agents are major culprits. Aripiprazole has the lowest risk among antipsychotics.

Antipsychotic and certain antidepressant weight gain and metabolic syndrome: weight gain, central adiposity, hyperglycemia, dyslipidemia. Olanzapine and clozapine are the highest risk; quetiapine and risperidone moderate; ziprasidone, lurasidone, aripiprazole, and brexpiprazole lower risk. Pharmacists monitoring patients on second-generation antipsychotics should track weight, glucose, and lipid panel periodically.

SIADH from drugs: SSRIs, carbamazepine, oxcarbazepine, chemotherapy agents like vincristine, antipsychotics. Presents with hyponatremia: mental status changes, headache, seizures in severe cases.

Diabetes insipidus from lithium: polyuria, polydipsia, dilute urine. Reversible early, may become irreversible with chronic lithium.

Drug-induced hyperglycemia: corticosteroids, atypical antipsychotics, thiazides at higher doses, beta-blockers (more so non-cardioselective), tacrolimus, cyclosporine, protease inhibitors. Monitor.

Drug-induced hypoglycemia: insulin and sulfonylureas obviously, plus sulfonamide antibiotics increasing sulfonylurea effect, fluoroquinolones, beta-blockers masking adrenergic warning signs.

Gynecomastia: spironolactone, digoxin, cimetidine, finasteride, ketoconazole, certain antiretrovirals, anabolic steroids on withdrawal.

Pharmacist Pearls

Pearl one. Palpate the thyroid in every visit for patients on amiodarone, lithium, or interferon. Track over time.

Pearl two. Annual diabetic foot exam, with monofilament testing, is a non-negotiable for any insulin-treated patient or any patient with longstanding diabetes.

Pearl three. Chronic steroid users need a written taper plan and stress-dose guidance.

Pearl four. New galactorrhea or amenorrhea on a second-generation antipsychotic is hyperprolactinemia until proven otherwise. Check prolactin.

Red Flags

Myxedema coma: profoundly hypothermic, bradycardic, somnolent hypothyroid patient. Emergency.

Thyroid storm: severe hyperthyroidism with fever, agitation, tachyarrhythmia. Emergency.

Adrenal crisis: hypotensive, hypoglycemic patient on chronic steroids or with primary adrenal insufficiency who recently became ill. Emergency, give IV hydrocortisone.

Hyperglycemic emergencies: DKA in type 1, HHS in type 2. Severe dehydration, altered mental status, severe hyperglycemia.

Severe hypoglycemia with altered consciousness in a sulfonylurea or insulin patient.

Diabetic foot ulcer with cellulitis, especially with crepitus or systemic signs: emergency, possible necrotizing infection.

Audio Quiz

Five questions.

Question one.

A patient on amiodarone for two years reports cold intolerance, fatigue, weight gain, and a hoarse voice. Reflexes show a "hung-up" relaxation phase. What is the diagnosis and the management consideration?

The answer: amiodarone-induced hypothyroidism. Check TSH and free T4. Levothyroxine replacement is generally safe and effective. Decision to continue amiodarone depends on the indication.

Question two.

A 65-year-old diabetic patient with reduced sensation to monofilament at three plantar sites and absent posterior tibial pulses. What is the clinical picture, and what counseling is non-negotiable?

The answer: combined diabetic neuropathy and PAD, a high-risk foot. Counseling must include daily inspection, properly fitted shoes, immediate reporting of any lesion, avoidance of barefoot walking, and same-day evaluation of new wounds.

Question three.

A patient on prednisone 15 mg daily for six months stopped the medication abruptly. He returns hypotensive, confused, and hyponatremic. What is the diagnosis and immediate management?

The answer: adrenal crisis from abruptly discontinued exogenous steroid. Give intravenous hydrocortisone immediately, support blood pressure with fluids, and refer for inpatient management.

Question four.

A young woman on risperidone reports milky nipple discharge and missed periods. What complication, and what is the management option?

The answer: antipsychotic-induced hyperprolactinemia. Check prolactin. Switch to a prolactin-sparing antipsychotic such as aripiprazole.

Question five.

A patient on lithium presents with new polyuria and polydipsia. Urine is dilute. Sodium is normal. What complication?

The answer: lithium-induced nephrogenic diabetes insipidus. Discuss with prescriber options including amiloride, lithium dose reduction, or alternative mood stabilizer if benefits permit.
