Episode Four. HEENT.

Welcome back. HEENT stands for head, eyes, ears, nose, and throat. The acronym groups together body regions that share examination tools, the otoscope and ophthalmoscope, and that yield enormous diagnostic information for the time you invest. As a pharmacist, you may not do a full HEENT exam at every visit, but you'll do pieces of it constantly: looking at pupils in a suspected opioid overdose, inspecting the oropharynx for thrush in an inhaled steroid user, palpating a thyroid in a patient on amiodarone, checking for nystagmus in a phenytoin patient.

Learning Objectives

One. Examine pupils for size, equality, and reactivity, and recognize the drug-related causes of miosis and mydriasis.

Two. Identify the basic external eye structures and recognize conjunctival pallor, scleral icterus, and corneal arcus.

Three. Use the ophthalmoscope at a basic level to visualize the red reflex and the optic disc.

Four. Use the otoscope to inspect the external auditory canal and the tympanic membrane.

Five. Perform a focused oral and oropharyngeal exam.

Six. Palpate the thyroid gland and recognize signs of dysfunction.

Inspection of the Head

Inspect the skull and scalp for symmetry, lumps, lacerations, and lesions. Note facial expression, symmetry of the nasolabial folds, and any obvious cranial nerve seven palsy. Listen briefly for bruits over the temporal and carotid arteries in an older patient with risk factors.

Two patterns to remember. The moon facies of Cushing's syndrome, a feature of chronic corticosteroid therapy: rounded face, central obesity, supraclavicular and dorsocervical fat pads. The cushingoid pharmacist patient is one of the most common medication-induced phenotypes you will see.

And the gaunt, prematurely aged face of chronic illness or severe malnutrition, common in advanced cancer, end-stage organ disease, and severe COPD.

The Eyes

We work outside-in.

External Eye

Look at the eyelids, conjunctiva, sclera, cornea, and iris.

Eyelids: ptosis, the drooping of the upper lid, can be physiologic, but new bilateral ptosis suggests myasthenia gravis. New unilateral ptosis with a small pupil and ipsilateral facial anhidrosis is Horner's syndrome and requires urgent evaluation.

Conjunctiva: pull down the lower lid and inspect. Pale conjunctivae suggest anemia. Bright redness with discharge suggests bacterial or viral conjunctivitis. Boggy edema, called chemosis, suggests allergy. A small subconjunctival hemorrhage is benign unless accompanied by other signs of bleeding diathesis.

Sclera: white in health. Yellow, called scleral icterus, indicates hyperbilirubinemia. The pharmacist relevance: drug-induced cholestasis from amoxicillin-clavulanate, statins, isoniazid, oral contraceptives, and certain anabolic steroids.

Cornea: a thin gray-white arc around the iris is corneal arcus. In patients under fifty, it suggests significant hyperlipidemia. After age sixty, it's usually a normal aging change.

Visual Acuity

Use a Snellen chart at twenty feet, or a near card at fourteen inches. Test each eye separately with their corrective lenses on. Twenty over twenty means the patient sees at twenty feet what a normal person sees at twenty feet. Twenty over forty means the patient must be at twenty feet to see what a normal person sees at forty.

Sudden vision loss is a red flag. Painless: think central retinal artery occlusion or vein occlusion. Painful: think acute angle-closure glaucoma, often precipitated by anticholinergic medications, including tricyclic antidepressants, certain antihistamines, and topiramate.

Visual Fields

Do a confrontation test. Sit two feet from the patient, eye level, each of you covering the opposite eye. Bring a wiggling finger in from each of the four quadrants midway between you. The patient should see the finger at the same moment you do. A patient who consistently misses one quadrant may have a hemianopia from a stroke, tumor, or chiasmal lesion.

Pupillary Exam

Pupils are described by size, shape, equality, and reactivity. Normal: round, three to five millimeters, equal, reactive to light directly and consensually.

Direct response: shine the light in one eye, that pupil constricts.

Consensual response: shine the light in one eye, the other pupil also constricts.

Accommodation: ask the patient to focus on a distant object, then on your finger close to their nose. Both pupils should constrict.

PERRLA. Pupils Equal, Round, Reactive to Light and Accommodation. The standard normal documentation.

Drug-related findings.

Miosis, pinpoint pupils. Opioids are the classic cause. Also cholinergic toxicity, like organophosphate exposure, and certain antipsychotics. Bilateral pinpoint pupils in a comatose patient: opioids until proven otherwise.

Mydriasis, dilated pupils. Anticholinergics, sympathomimetics like cocaine and amphetamines, withdrawal from opioids, and brain death. Fixed dilated pupils unilaterally in an awake patient suggest a third cranial nerve palsy, often from compression and urgent.

Anisocoria, unequal pupils. A small difference of less than one millimeter is normal in twenty percent of people. New, marked anisocoria is a red flag.

Ophthalmoscopy: The Essentials for Pharmacists

You will not become an expert ophthalmoscopist in this course, and you don't need to. Two findings to look for.

The red reflex. From about a foot away, shine the ophthalmoscope light through the pupil. You should see a uniform red-orange glow. Loss of red reflex in one eye in a child suggests retinoblastoma. In an adult, cataract or hemorrhage. A leukocoria, white reflex, in any patient is always abnormal.

The optic disc. Slightly off-center, about three to four millimeters across, with a sharp orange edge and a central cup. Look for: a swollen disc with blurred margins, called papilledema, which indicates elevated intracranial pressure and is a medical emergency.

Drug-associated retinal findings to be aware of, even if you don't visualize them yourself: hydroxychloroquine retinopathy, requiring annual screening after five years of use; ethambutol optic neuropathy; tamoxifen retinopathy; vigabatrin visual field defects; digoxin xanthopsia, the yellow-green vision of digoxin toxicity.

The Ears

Inspect the auricle for shape, lesions, and the post-auricular area for swelling.

Otoscopy. Use the largest speculum that comfortably fits. In adults, pull the auricle up and back to straighten the canal. In children, pull down and back. Brace the otoscope with your fifth finger against the patient's cheek so that any sudden movement doesn't drive the speculum into the canal.

Inspect the canal for cerumen, foreign bodies, redness, or discharge. Then the tympanic membrane. A normal TM is pearly gray, translucent, with a visible cone of light reflex in the anterior-inferior quadrant. The malleus handle is visible through the membrane.

Findings.

Red, bulging TM with loss of landmarks: acute otitis media.

Retracted TM: chronic eustachian tube dysfunction.

Air-fluid level or bubbles behind the TM: serous otitis media.

Perforation: a hole, sometimes with discharge.

Hearing

Whispered voice test or finger-rub at arm's length. Each ear separately. If hearing loss is detected, do Weber and Rinne if your scope of practice allows.

Drug-induced ototoxicity is a real concern in pharmacy. Aminoglycosides like gentamicin and tobramycin: irreversible damage, more vestibular than cochlear, dose- and trough-related. Loop diuretics in high doses, especially IV: usually reversible. Cisplatin: dose-related, often permanent. High-dose salicylates: reversible tinnitus and hearing loss. Macrolides at high doses: reversible.

Counsel any patient on these drugs to report new tinnitus, hearing change, or dizziness immediately. Tinnitus is often the earliest warning.

The Nose

Inspect the external nose for symmetry and lesions. Tilt the patient's head back, lift the tip of the nose, and use the otoscope or a penlight to view the nasal cavity.

Findings. Pale, boggy turbinates with clear discharge: allergic rhinitis. Erythematous turbinates with purulent discharge: viral or bacterial infection. Septal deviation. Polyps, pale grape-like growths, often in patients with chronic rhinitis or aspirin-exacerbated respiratory disease.

The pharmacist relevance. Chronic intranasal cocaine causes septal ulceration and eventually perforation. Excessive use of decongestant nasal sprays for more than three days produces rhinitis medicamentosa, a rebound congestion that perpetuates the cycle of use.

The Oral Exam

Lips: color, moisture, lesions. Angular cheilitis at the corners suggests B-vitamin or iron deficiency, or chronic drooling and yeast overgrowth.

Buccal mucosa: pink, moist. Look for Koplik spots, the small white spots opposite the lower molars in early measles; for the lacy white lines of lichen planus; for the white plaques of candidiasis, which are scrapeable; for the white non-scrapeable plaques of leukoplakia, which can be premalignant.

Tongue: a normal tongue is pink and moist, with papillae. A smooth, red, depapillated tongue suggests B12 or iron deficiency. A black, hairy appearance is often from broad-spectrum antibiotics, smoking, or chlorhexidine. Macroglossia, an enlarged tongue, in hypothyroidism, acromegaly, amyloidosis.

Gums: gingival hyperplasia is a classic finding with phenytoin, cyclosporine, and certain calcium channel blockers, especially nifedipine. Inspect for bleeding, recession, and dental hygiene.

Teeth: tetracyclines given to children under eight produce permanent yellow-brown discoloration. Chronic acid reflux or bulimia erodes the lingual surfaces.

Oropharynx: depress the tongue, ask the patient to say "ahh." Inspect tonsils, posterior pharynx, and uvula. Tonsillar exudate plus fever plus tender anterior cervical nodes plus absence of cough is the classic Centor pattern for streptococcal pharyngitis.

The drug findings to flag: white curd-like patches on the tongue, palate, or buccal mucosa in a patient using an inhaled corticosteroid. This is oral candidiasis from poor inhaler technique and inadequate mouth rinsing. Counsel patients to rinse and spit after every inhaled corticosteroid dose.

The Neck and Thyroid

Inspect the neck for symmetry and visible masses. Have the patient swallow water; a thyroid mass moves up with the swallow because it is attached to the trachea.

Palpate the thyroid. Two approaches.

The anterior approach: stand in front of the patient, use the pads of your fingers to feel both lobes, asking the patient to swallow.

The posterior approach: stand behind the patient, reach around with both hands, find the cricoid cartilage as a landmark, and palpate the isthmus just below it and each lobe lateral to the trachea, asking the patient to swallow.

Normal: the thyroid is often not palpable, or feels smooth, symmetric, soft, and non-tender. Findings: a diffusely enlarged thyroid is a goiter. A discrete nodule needs investigation. Tenderness suggests thyroiditis.

Drug relevance. Amiodarone has high iodine content and causes both hyperthyroidism and hypothyroidism. Lithium causes hypothyroidism and goiter. Interferon causes thyroiditis. PD-1 inhibitor immune checkpoint drugs cause endocrine adverse effects including thyroiditis. Pharmacists managing these patients should ask about and look for thyroid signs at each visit.

Cervical Lymph Nodes

Palpate the cervical chains using the pads of the fingers in a circular motion. Anterior cervical, posterior cervical, submandibular, submental, tonsillar, preauricular, postauricular, occipital, and supraclavicular.

Note size, tenderness, mobility, and consistency. Small, mobile, soft, tender nodes suggest reactive lymphadenopathy from infection. Hard, fixed, non-tender nodes are concerning for malignancy. A supraclavicular node, called Virchow's node when left-sided, suggests intra-abdominal malignancy.

Pharmacist Pearls

Pearl one. Inhaled corticosteroid users should rinse their mouth and spit after each dose to prevent thrush. Show this to every new ICS patient at dispensing.

Pearl two. Pinpoint pupils plus respiratory depression equals opioid until proven otherwise. Give naloxone.

Pearl three. Patients on amiodarone, lithium, or immune checkpoint inhibitors need periodic thyroid checks. You can palpate at every visit, in seconds.

Pearl four. Hydroxychloroquine retinopathy is preventable with adherence to baseline and annual ophthalmologic exams. Pharmacists can prompt this scheduling at every refill.

Red Flags

Sudden vision loss. Sudden severe eye pain. Acute red eye with photophobia. Asymmetric pupils with altered mental status. Papilledema. New diplopia. Acute severe hearing loss. Posterior cervical or supraclavicular hard fixed lymph node. Oropharyngeal exudate plus inability to tolerate liquids, drooling, or trismus.

Audio Quiz

Five questions.

Question one.

A patient on long-term hydroxychloroquine for lupus comes in for a refill. What screening should you confirm is up to date?

The answer: annual ophthalmologic exam after five years of therapy, sometimes sooner if risk factors. Hydroxychloroquine retinopathy is irreversible but largely preventable with proper screening.

Question two.

A comatose patient is brought in with pinpoint pupils and a respiratory rate of six. What is your immediate intervention?

The answer: naloxone administration. The constellation of miosis and respiratory depression is opioid toxicity until proven otherwise. Establish airway support and give naloxone in titrated doses.

Question three.

A patient using fluticasone inhaler reports white patches on his tongue and palate that come off when scraped. What is the finding, and how is it prevented?

The answer: oral candidiasis. Prevented by rinsing the mouth and spitting after each inhaled corticosteroid dose, and by ensuring proper inhaler technique, ideally with a spacer.

Question four.

A patient on amiodarone for atrial fibrillation has lost ten pounds, reports palpitations, and feels warm. What thyroid finding might you elicit, and what is going on?

The answer: a possibly enlarged or tender thyroid suggesting amiodarone-induced thyrotoxicosis. Amiodarone has high iodine content and can cause both hyperthyroidism and hypothyroidism. Check TSH and free T4, and discuss next steps with the prescriber.

Question five.

A child swallowed liquid from a bottle in the medicine cabinet. He presents with hot, dry, flushed skin, dilated pupils, tachycardia, and confused speech. What toxidrome and what likely class of medication?

The answer: anticholinergic toxidrome. Hot as a hare, dry as a bone, red as a beet, blind as a bat, mad as a hatter. Common culprits in a home: diphenhydramine, dimenhydrinate, certain tricyclic antidepressants, scopolamine, and some antimuscarinics for overactive bladder.
