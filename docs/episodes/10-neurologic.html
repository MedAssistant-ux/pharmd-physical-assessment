Episode Ten. The Neurologic Exam.

Welcome back. The neurologic exam is the most intimidating system for most students and the most rewarding to master. Done well, it localizes a lesion within minutes. Done poorly, it misses strokes and toxic encephalopathies. For pharmacists, the neurologic exam reveals everything from benzodiazepine sedation to anticholinergic delirium to lithium toxicity. Today we'll walk through the six components and the drug correlations.

Learning Objectives

One. Assess mental status, including level of consciousness, orientation, attention, memory, and language.

Two. Screen the cranial nerves systematically.

Three. Evaluate motor function: bulk, tone, strength, and abnormal movements.

Four. Test sensory function across the modalities.

Five. Elicit and grade deep tendon reflexes.

Six. Test coordination and gait.

Mental Status

Three levels.

Level of consciousness, from alert to comatose. Use the standard descriptors: alert, lethargic (arouses easily, drifts back), obtunded (arouses with vigorous stimulation), stuporous (arouses only with painful stimulation), comatose (no arousal). The Glasgow Coma Scale quantifies this with eye, verbal, and motor scores summed from three to fifteen.

Orientation: person, place, time, situation. Often abbreviated A and O times four. Drug-related disorientation: anticholinergics, benzodiazepines, opioids, sedative-hypnotics, lithium toxicity, sodium derangements.

Higher cognitive function: attention, memory, language, executive function, visuospatial. A formal screen is the Montreal Cognitive Assessment, the MoCA, or the simpler Mini-Cog: recall three words after a clock drawing and naming exercise. The Mini-Mental State Examination is older and trademark-restricted.

Delirium versus dementia.

Delirium is acute onset, hours to days, with fluctuating course, prominent inattention, often visual hallucinations. The acuity is the key word. A new behavioral change in a hospitalized patient with multiple medications is delirium until proven otherwise.

Dementia is chronic onset, months to years, with stable or slowly progressive course, preserved attention early, and prominent memory loss.

A patient with dementia can develop superimposed delirium. The acute change is the delirium.

The Cranial Nerves

Twelve nerves, twelve quick tests. You can screen all twelve in two to three minutes.

CN I, olfactory: have the patient close eyes and identify a familiar smell, coffee or peppermint, one nostril at a time. Often skipped in routine exams. Lost in early Parkinson's disease, head trauma.

CN II, optic: visual acuity, visual fields by confrontation, fundoscopy if indicated.

CN III, IV, VI, extraocular movements: have the patient follow your finger through the H pattern of cardinal gaze. Note any diplopia, nystagmus, or limited movement. CN III also controls pupil constriction and the levator palpebrae (eyelid). A "down and out" eye with ptosis and a dilated pupil is a complete third nerve palsy.

Nystagmus is rhythmic eye oscillation. Drug-induced nystagmus is classic for phenytoin (gaze-evoked), carbamazepine, alcohol, lithium, and certain anticonvulsants.

CN V, trigeminal: light touch sensation to the forehead (V1), cheek (V2), and jaw (V3) on both sides. Motor: have the patient clench the jaw; palpate the masseters for symmetric contraction.

CN VII, facial: have the patient raise eyebrows, close eyes tightly, show teeth in a smile, puff cheeks. Forehead-sparing weakness with lower face weakness is central, suggesting a stroke. Whole-side facial weakness including forehead is peripheral, classically Bell's palsy.

CN VIII, vestibulocochlear: hearing as discussed in HEENT. Vestibular function: head impulse, nystagmus, test of skew (HINTS) for distinguishing peripheral vertigo from posterior circulation stroke.

CN IX and X, glossopharyngeal and vagus: have the patient say "ahh," watch the uvula rise in the midline. A uvula that deviates to one side suggests vagus injury on the opposite side. Gag reflex if needed.

CN XI, spinal accessory: shrug shoulders against resistance for trapezius. Turn head against resistance for sternocleidomastoid.

CN XII, hypoglossal: stick out the tongue. Look for deviation, atrophy, fasciculations. The tongue deviates toward the side of the lesion. Lithium and certain antipsychotics can cause tongue fasciculations or tardive lingual dyskinesia.

Motor Exam

Inspect for muscle bulk: atrophy or hypertrophy. Fasciculations, the small involuntary twitches under the skin, can be benign (post-exertion in calves) or pathologic (lower motor neuron disease).

Tone: passively move the limb through its range of motion. Spasticity: velocity-dependent increase in tone, classic for upper motor neuron lesion. Rigidity: constant resistance throughout the range, classic for Parkinson's. Cogwheel rigidity: rigidity with superimposed tremor. Lead-pipe rigidity: severe, smooth resistance, seen in neuroleptic malignant syndrome.

Strength: graded zero to five.

Zero: no contraction.

One: flicker of contraction, no movement.

Two: movement with gravity eliminated.

Three: movement against gravity but not against resistance.

Four: movement against some resistance, less than normal.

Five: full strength.

Test major muscle groups: shoulder abduction and adduction, elbow flexion and extension, wrist extension, finger abduction, hip flexion and extension, knee flexion and extension, ankle dorsiflexion and plantar flexion.

Pronator drift: have the patient hold both arms out in front, palms up, eyes closed, for ten seconds. Subtle pronation and downward drift of one arm is a sensitive sign of upper motor neuron weakness or proprioceptive loss.

Sensory Exam

Test the major modalities, comparing side to side.

Light touch: wisp of cotton.

Pinprick: a safety pin, used judiciously.

Vibration: a 128 Hz tuning fork applied to a bony prominence, typically the great toe and the medial malleolus. Loss of vibration sense in the feet is one of the earliest signs of peripheral neuropathy, including diabetic neuropathy and B12-deficient neuropathy.

Proprioception: passively move the great toe up or down; ask the patient which way it moved.

Two-point discrimination and graphesthesia: cortical sensory modalities.

Sensory level: a level below which sensation is reduced — think spinal cord lesion. Glove-and-stocking distribution with distal-symmetric loss: peripheral neuropathy.

Reflexes

Deep tendon reflexes are graded zero to four.

Zero: absent.

One plus: diminished.

Two plus: normal.

Three plus: brisk, may be normal or pathologic depending on context.

Four plus: hyperactive with clonus.

The major reflexes you'll test.

Biceps: C5, C6.

Brachioradialis: C5, C6.

Triceps: C7, C8.

Patellar: L3, L4.

Achilles: S1, S2.

Babinski reflex, also called the plantar response. Stroke the lateral sole from heel to ball, curving across to the great toe. The normal adult response is flexion (downgoing toes). An extensor response with great toe extension and fanning of the other toes is Babinski-positive, indicating upper motor neuron involvement.

Hyperreflexia with sustained clonus and Babinski signs: classic UMN. Generalized hyperreflexia plus clonus plus hyperthermia plus clonus plus agitation in a patient on serotonergic drugs: serotonin syndrome.

Coordination and Gait

Cerebellar tests.

Finger-to-nose: have the patient touch your finger then their nose, repeatedly, in time. Dysmetria suggests cerebellar dysfunction. Intention tremor that worsens as the patient nears the target: cerebellar.

Heel-to-shin: with the patient supine, have them place one heel on the opposite knee and run it down the shin smoothly. Cerebellar lesions produce dysmetric, jerky movement.

Rapid alternating movements: rapidly pronate and supinate the hand on the thigh. Dysdiadochokinesia, the loss of smooth alternation, suggests cerebellar dysfunction.

Romberg test: have the patient stand with feet together, arms at sides, eyes open, then closed. A patient who sways or falls with eyes closed but not open has positive Romberg, indicating proprioceptive loss (dorsal column dysfunction or peripheral neuropathy). A patient who falls with eyes open has cerebellar disease.

Gait: as covered in musculoskeletal. Drug-related gait disorders: ataxia from phenytoin or lithium, shuffling parkinsonism from antipsychotics or metoclopramide, drug-induced peripheral neuropathy gait from chemotherapy.

Drug-Induced Neurologic Findings

Tremors.

Resting tremor: at rest, improves with action. Parkinson's disease classically. Drug-induced: antipsychotics, metoclopramide.

Action tremor: with movement. Essential tremor. Drug-induced: lithium, valproate, beta-agonists, caffeine, levothyroxine excess, SSRIs.

Postural tremor: with sustained posture. Often essential or drug-induced.

Encephalopathy: confusion, slowed cognition, fluctuating attention. Causes: opioids, benzodiazepines, anticholinergics, lithium, valproate (hyperammonemia), antibiotics like cefepime in renal failure, sedatives generally.

Seizures: lowered seizure threshold by bupropion (especially immediate-release at high doses), tramadol, quinolones in epileptic patients, clozapine, sudden withdrawal of benzodiazepines, alcohol withdrawal.

Extrapyramidal symptoms from dopamine blockade.

Acute dystonia: sudden involuntary contraction, often torticollis or oculogyric crisis. Treat with IM/IV benztropine or diphenhydramine.

Akathisia: subjective restlessness, inability to stay still. Often misdiagnosed as anxiety.

Parkinsonism: bradykinesia, rigidity, tremor.

Tardive dyskinesia: involuntary repetitive movements of the face and tongue, late onset, often irreversible.

Peripheral neuropathy: chemotherapy (vincristine, paclitaxel, bortezomib, platinum agents), isoniazid (preventable with pyridoxine), metronidazole at high dose, fluoroquinolones, statins rarely, amiodarone.

Pharmacist Pearls

Pearl one. Acute change in mental status in any patient on opioids, benzodiazepines, anticholinergics, or recently started antibiotics: assume drug effect first.

Pearl two. Bilateral hyperreflexia with clonus plus tremor plus agitation plus hyperthermia plus diaphoresis in a patient on serotonergic medications: serotonin syndrome until proven otherwise. Stop serotonergic agents and treat.

Pearl three. A new tremor in a patient on lithium plus dehydration, plus NSAIDs or ACE inhibitors: think lithium toxicity. Check level.

Pearl four. Akathisia is often mistaken for anxiety. A patient on an antipsychotic who paces and can't sit still has akathisia until proven otherwise. The treatment is dose reduction and a beta-blocker like propranolol.

Red Flags

Acute focal neurologic deficit: stroke until proven otherwise. Time is brain.

Sudden severe headache, especially "worst of life" or thunderclap: subarachnoid hemorrhage.

Altered mental status with fever and neck stiffness: meningitis.

Acute change in mental status with fluctuating consciousness on multiple sedating medications: drug-induced delirium.

Bilateral lower extremity weakness with bowel and bladder dysfunction and saddle anesthesia: cauda equina.

Audio Quiz

Five questions.

Question one.

A patient on lithium presents with new tremor, ataxia, confusion, and a recent course of lisinopril and ibuprofen for back pain. What complication, and what is the mechanism?

The answer: lithium toxicity. Both ACE inhibitors and NSAIDs reduce renal lithium clearance and raise serum lithium levels, especially in dehydrated patients. Check the lithium level, hold the offending drugs, and refer if level is significantly elevated.

Question two.

A patient on linezolid is started on tramadol after an injury. Forty-eight hours later he is agitated, sweating, has clonus, hyperreflexia, and a temperature of 39 degrees. What is the diagnosis?

The answer: serotonin syndrome. Linezolid is a weak monoamine oxidase inhibitor and tramadol has serotonergic activity. Stop both agents, provide aggressive supportive care, and consider cyproheptadine if severe.

Question three.

A patient on haloperidol presents with sudden involuntary contraction pulling the neck to one side. What is the syndrome and the immediate treatment?

The answer: acute dystonic reaction. Treat with intramuscular or intravenous benztropine or diphenhydramine; symptoms typically resolve within minutes. Consider an alternative antipsychotic going forward.

Question four.

A patient on metronidazole at high dose for thirty days presents with tingling and burning in both feet, with reduced vibration sense to the ankles. What is the likely complication?

The answer: metronidazole-induced peripheral neuropathy, related to cumulative dose. Stop or reduce metronidazole; recovery is typically partial and slow.

Question five.

A 70-year-old admitted patient on multiple medications including diphenhydramine for sleep, oxybutynin for bladder, and a recent prescription of cefepime in the setting of acute kidney injury, becomes confused with visual hallucinations and agitation. What is the diagnosis and what should you address?

The answer: drug-induced delirium with two contributors. Anticholinergic burden from diphenhydramine and oxybutynin, plus cefepime neurotoxicity from accumulation in renal impairment. Stop or reduce the anticholinergics, adjust the cefepime dose to renal function or change agents, and treat delirium with non-pharmacologic measures.
