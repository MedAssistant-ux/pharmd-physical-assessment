Episode Six. The Cardiovascular Exam.

Welcome back. The cardiac exam intimidates students more than any other system. The truth is, the basics are not complicated. Two normal sounds. Two extra sounds. Four classic murmurs at four specific locations. A few inspection findings. Master that, and you can perform a clinically useful cardiac exam for ninety percent of pharmacist encounters.

Learning Objectives

One. Identify the four cardiac auscultation areas and what each represents.

Two. Recognize S1 and S2, distinguish them, and identify physiologic versus pathologic splitting.

Three. Recognize S3 and S4 and their clinical significance.

Four. Identify the four classic murmurs: aortic stenosis, aortic regurgitation, mitral regurgitation, and mitral stenosis.

Five. Estimate jugular venous pressure and interpret it.

Six. Evaluate peripheral edema and integrate cardiac findings into a clinical picture.

Inspection and Palpation

Stand at the foot of the bed first. Look. Is the patient comfortable? Sitting bolt upright suggests orthopnea, a sign of heart failure. Are there visible pulsations of the carotid arteries? Tracheal deviation? Surgical scars from prior cardiac surgery?

Move to the chest. Inspect the precordium. A visible apical impulse at the fifth intercostal space, midclavicular line, is normal in thin patients. A visible heave to the left of the sternum suggests right ventricular pressure overload.

Palpate. Find the apical impulse, also called the point of maximal impulse, the PMI. Normally fifth intercostal space, just inside the midclavicular line, the size of a dime, lasting less than the first two-thirds of systole. Displaced laterally and inferiorly: cardiomegaly. Sustained beyond the first two-thirds of systole: pressure overload, as in aortic stenosis or hypertension. Hyperdynamic, vigorous: volume overload or thyrotoxicosis.

Palpate the second intercostal spaces, right and left of the sternum. A palpable second heart sound suggests pulmonary or systemic hypertension. Palpate the lower left sternal border for a right ventricular heave. Palpate for thrills, palpable vibrations from very loud murmurs.

Auscultation: The Four Areas

Memorize these locations. They are not where the valves actually sit; they are where the sound radiates best.

Aortic area: right second intercostal space, just right of the sternum. Best for aortic valve sounds, particularly aortic stenosis murmur.

Pulmonic area: left second intercostal space, just left of the sternum. Best for pulmonic valve sounds.

Erb's point: left third intercostal space, just left of the sternum. Good for aortic regurgitation.

Tricuspid area: left lower sternal border, fourth or fifth intercostal space. Best for tricuspid valve sounds and ventricular gallops.

Mitral area: left fifth intercostal space, midclavicular line, which is the apex. Best for mitral valve sounds, including mitral regurgitation murmur and S3.

Listen at each area, first with the diaphragm pressed firmly for high-pitched sounds, then with the bell rested lightly for low-pitched sounds.

Normal Heart Sounds: S1 and S2

S1 is the closure of the mitral and tricuspid valves at the start of systole. It corresponds to the carotid pulse upstroke and is loudest at the apex.

S2 is the closure of the aortic and pulmonic valves at the end of systole. It is loudest at the base, the second intercostal spaces.

To orient yourself when listening: palpate the carotid pulse with one hand while you listen. The sound you hear at the same moment as the carotid pulse is S1.

S2 can have a physiologic split, where aortic closure precedes pulmonic closure by a few milliseconds during inspiration. You hear "lub Da-da" instead of "lub dub." Inspiration widens the split because inspiration delays pulmonic closure. This is normal in young, healthy patients.

A fixed split S2, one that doesn't change with respiration, suggests atrial septal defect. A paradoxical split, where the split widens on expiration, suggests aortic stenosis or left bundle branch block.

S3 and S4: The Extra Sounds

S3 is an early diastolic sound. Low-pitched, heard best at the apex with the bell with the patient in left lateral decubitus position. It occurs just after S2 and creates a triple cadence sometimes described as "Kentucky": "lub-dub-ta." The "Kentucky" mnemonic places the third syllable in the right spot.

In young patients under thirty, an S3 can be normal. In older adults, an S3 is almost always pathologic and signals volume overload. Heart failure with reduced ejection fraction is the classic cause. Mitral regurgitation, ventricular septal defect, and high-output states like anemia and thyrotoxicosis can also produce it.

S4 is a late diastolic sound, occurring just before S1. The triple cadence sounds like "Tennessee": "ta-lub-dub." It reflects atrial contraction into a stiff, non-compliant ventricle. Causes: left ventricular hypertrophy from longstanding hypertension, aortic stenosis, hypertrophic cardiomyopathy, acute myocardial infarction. S4 is never normal in adults.

The pearl. S3 says volume. S4 says stiffness. Both heard at the apex, both low-pitched, both with the bell.

Murmurs: A Systematic Description

For every murmur you hear, describe seven features. Use this acronym: SCRIPT-CG.

S for Site, where you hear it best.

C for Configuration: crescendo, decrescendo, crescendo-decrescendo (diamond shaped), or holosystolic (uniform).

R for Radiation, where the sound transmits.

I for Intensity: graded one through six. Grade one is barely audible. Grade two is faint but easy. Grade three is moderately loud, no thrill. Grade four is loud with a palpable thrill. Grade five is loud, audible with stethoscope edge on chest. Grade six audible without stethoscope.

P for Pitch.

T for Timing: systolic or diastolic.

C for Character: harsh, blowing, rumbling, musical.

G for Grade, again. The intensity scale.

The Four Classic Murmurs

These are the murmurs every clinician must recognize. Master these four, and you cover the vast majority of common valve disease.

Aortic Stenosis

Site: aortic area, right second intercostal space.

Configuration: crescendo-decrescendo, diamond-shaped.

Radiation: to the carotids bilaterally.

Timing: midsystolic.

Character: harsh.

Associated findings: a single or paradoxically split S2, a sustained PMI, narrow pulse pressure, slow-rising "parvus et tardus" carotid upstroke. Older patient with exertional syncope, angina, or dyspnea: think aortic stenosis. Severe AS is a contraindication to vasodilators because afterload reduction collapses cardiac output.

Aortic Regurgitation

Site: Erb's point, left third intercostal space.

Configuration: decrescendo.

Radiation: down the left sternal border.

Timing: early diastolic, immediately after S2.

Character: high-pitched, blowing. Heard best with the diaphragm with the patient sitting up, leaning forward, breath held in expiration.

Associated findings: wide pulse pressure, bounding pulses (water-hammer), head bobbing in severe cases. Causes include bicuspid aortic valve, hypertension, infective endocarditis, aortic dissection, and connective tissue disease.

Mitral Regurgitation

Site: apex, mitral area.

Configuration: holosystolic, uniform throughout systole.

Radiation: to the axilla.

Timing: holosystolic.

Character: blowing, high-pitched. Heard best with the diaphragm at the apex.

Associated findings: S3, displaced PMI in chronic MR. Causes include mitral valve prolapse, ischemic papillary muscle dysfunction, rheumatic disease, and infective endocarditis.

Mitral Stenosis

Site: apex.

Configuration: opening snap followed by mid-diastolic rumble with presystolic accentuation.

Radiation: minimal.

Timing: diastolic.

Character: low-pitched, rumbling. Heard best with the bell at the apex with the patient in left lateral decubitus position, after exertion if subtle.

Associated findings: loud S1 from a doming valve, opening snap. Almost always rheumatic in origin globally. Atrial fibrillation common. Hemoptysis can occur.

Other Murmurs Worth Knowing

Tricuspid regurgitation: holosystolic at the lower left sternal border, increasing with inspiration (Carvallo sign). Associated with right heart failure, pulmonary hypertension, endocarditis from IV drug use.

Pulmonic stenosis: crescendo-decrescendo at the pulmonic area, often congenital.

Innocent murmurs: soft, midsystolic, no associated symptoms, normal cardiac exam otherwise, frequent in children and pregnant women.

Jugular Venous Pressure

The JVP is the pharmacist's window into right heart filling pressures.

Position the patient at thirty to forty-five degrees of head elevation. Turn the head slightly to the left. Use tangential light. Locate the right internal jugular vein, which sits behind the sternocleidomastoid muscle and shows as a soft, biphasic, non-palpable pulsation in the neck, in contrast to the single, palpable carotid pulse.

Measure: find the highest oscillation point of the JVP. Drop a horizontal line from there to the sternal angle, which is roughly five centimeters above the right atrium in any position. Add the vertical distance in centimeters to five.

Normal JVP is less than eight or nine centimeters of water. Elevated JVP suggests volume overload, right heart failure, tricuspid regurgitation, pericardial disease, or pulmonary hypertension.

Kussmaul's sign: paradoxical rise of JVP with inspiration. Seen in constrictive pericarditis and severe right heart failure.

Hepatojugular reflux: sustained pressure on the right upper quadrant for ten to thirty seconds causes JVP to rise by more than three centimeters and stay elevated. Suggests right heart failure.

Peripheral Edema

Press your thumb firmly into the pretibial area for five to ten seconds. Lift. Observe the depression and its persistence.

Grading.

One plus: barely detectable pit, disappears rapidly.

Two plus: slight indentation, four to fifteen seconds to disappear.

Three plus: deeper pit, persists more than a minute.

Four plus: very deep pit, lasts two to five minutes.

Bilateral lower-extremity edema with cardiac findings (S3, elevated JVP, basal crackles): heart failure.

Unilateral edema, especially with warmth, tenderness, or a positive Homan's sign: DVT.

Bilateral pedal edema without cardiac findings: dihydropyridine calcium channel blocker like amlodipine, NSAID-induced sodium retention, venous insufficiency, hepatic or renal failure, severe hypoalbuminemia.

Drug-Related Cardiovascular Findings

Bradycardia and AV block: beta-blockers, non-dihydropyridine calcium channel blockers (diltiazem, verapamil), digoxin, amiodarone, ivabradine, clonidine.

Tachycardia: albuterol, theophylline, levothyroxine excess, sympathomimetics, stimulants, dehydration from diuretics, anticholinergics.

QT prolongation, manifesting as syncope or torsades: many antibiotics (macrolides, fluoroquinolones), antipsychotics (haloperidol, ziprasidone), antiarrhythmics, methadone, ondansetron.

Hypotension with positional change: alpha-blockers, vasodilators, diuretics, tricyclics, nitrates, certain antipsychotics.

Edema: dihydropyridine calcium channel blockers, NSAIDs, thiazolidinediones, gabapentin/pregabalin at high doses.

Pharmacist Pearls

Pearl one. Always palpate the carotid pulse while you auscultate. It anchors you to S1 every time and prevents misidentifying systole and diastole.

Pearl two. The classic heart failure exam is S3 plus elevated JVP plus bibasilar crackles plus pedal edema. Document all four positively or negatively.

Pearl three. A patient with severe aortic stenosis given a nitrate or other significant afterload-reducer can collapse. Know the murmur before you recommend the vasodilator.

Pearl four. New bradycardia plus a beta-blocker plus a CCM is a common cause of falls in elderly patients on dual rate control. Question the indication for combined therapy.

Red Flags

Acute severe chest pain. New systolic murmur after MI, suggesting papillary muscle rupture or ventricular septal defect. New diastolic murmur with fever, suggesting endocarditis. JVP to the angle of the jaw with hypotension, suggesting cardiogenic shock or tamponade. Heart rate less than forty with symptoms. Heart rate greater than 150 sustained. Syncope with effort, in a patient with a midsystolic murmur — think aortic stenosis or hypertrophic cardiomyopathy.

Audio Quiz

Five questions.

Question one.

A 72-year-old presents with exertional syncope. On exam you hear a harsh crescendo-decrescendo systolic murmur at the right second intercostal space radiating to the carotids, with a slow-rising carotid upstroke. What is the diagnosis and what should you avoid prescribing?

The answer: severe aortic stenosis. Avoid nitrates and other significant vasodilators, which can drop afterload and collapse cardiac output. Refer for echocardiography and consideration of valve replacement.

Question two.

A patient with heart failure has an audible extra sound at the apex, low-pitched, just after S2, producing a "Kentucky" cadence. What is the sound and what does it signify?

The answer: S3 gallop, indicating ventricular volume overload. In an adult, this is almost always pathologic and consistent with decompensated heart failure. It signals a need for more aggressive diuresis or other intervention.

Question three.

A patient on amlodipine 10 mg daily for hypertension reports bilateral ankle swelling. Cardiac auscultation is normal. JVP is not elevated. Lung exam is clean. What is the likely cause?

The answer: dihydropyridine calcium channel blocker-induced peripheral edema. This is not heart failure. The mechanism is preferential arteriolar dilation increasing capillary hydrostatic pressure. Options include adding an ACE inhibitor or ARB, which counteract this effect, switching to a non-dihydropyridine, or reducing the dose.

Question four.

A patient newly started on azithromycin and ondansetron after surgery develops syncope. The ECG shows a long QT. Why is this combination problematic?

The answer: both azithromycin and ondansetron prolong the QT interval. Their additive effect can precipitate torsades de pointes, particularly in the setting of electrolyte abnormalities like hypokalemia or hypomagnesemia common after surgery. Avoid the combination when possible, correct electrolytes, and consider alternatives.

Question five.

You auscultate a holosystolic, blowing murmur at the apex that radiates to the axilla. What is the diagnosis and what other heart sound might you also hear in significant cases?

The answer: mitral regurgitation. In significant chronic MR, you may also hear an S3 from the volume-overloaded left ventricle, along with a displaced and hyperdynamic PMI.
