Episode Twelve. Genitourinary Considerations.

Welcome back. Pharmacist scope in genitourinary exam is intentionally limited. We do not perform pelvic, breast, rectal, or genital examinations except under specific authorization. But there is plenty within scope that affects pharmacotherapy. Today we'll cover what you do, what you don't, and the drug-related GU findings you need to recognize.

Learning Objectives

One. Assess costovertebral angle tenderness.

Two. Inspect for bladder distention and edema in renal disease.

Three. Take a focused BPH and incontinence history.

Four. Recognize the cutaneous and constitutional signs of urinary tract infection.

Five. Identify drug-induced urinary syndromes.

What's In Scope

A pharmacist can.

Take a focused urinary history.

Palpate the suprapubic area to assess for bladder distention.

Percuss for bladder dullness.

Strike the costovertebral angle to test for tenderness.

Inspect the lower extremities and sacrum for the edema and skin changes of chronic kidney disease.

Review medication lists and identify drugs that affect urinary symptoms or kidney function.

Counsel on adherence with pelvic floor exercises and lifestyle modifications.

A pharmacist generally does not, without specific authorization.

Perform a digital rectal exam.

Perform a pelvic exam.

Examine external genitalia.

Catheterize a patient.

Costovertebral Angle Tenderness

The costovertebral angle, or CVA, is the angle formed by the twelfth rib and the spine, overlying the kidney. With the patient sitting upright, place the palm of one hand over the CVA and strike that hand firmly with the ulnar fist of the other hand. Repeat on the opposite side.

A normal patient feels a thud without pain. A patient with pyelonephritis or nephrolithiasis often feels sharp, sometimes excruciating pain on the affected side. This is a quick, useful screen at the pharmacy bench.

Pharmacist relevance. A diabetic patient on a SGLT2 inhibitor with new fever, dysuria, and unilateral CVA tenderness: think pyelonephritis. SGLT2 inhibitors have been associated with urinary tract infections progressing to pyelonephritis in rare cases. Refer for urgent evaluation, urine culture, and possible imaging.

Bladder Assessment

The empty bladder is non-palpable and non-percussible behind the pubic symphysis. A distended bladder is palpable as a smooth, firm mass rising above the symphysis, and is dull to percussion.

Acute urinary retention. Suprapubic pain, palpable bladder, sometimes inability to void. Drug causes: anticholinergics, opioids, alpha-agonists, antipsychotics, antihistamines, certain antidepressants. Older men with BPH are at highest risk. Pharmacists should counsel on these risks at dispensing and consider alternatives in vulnerable patients.

BPH and Incontinence: The Pharmacist's Symptom Review

Use the International Prostate Symptom Score, the IPSS. Seven questions, scored zero to five each, plus a bother score.

The seven domains.

Incomplete emptying.

Frequency, urinating again less than two hours after the last urination.

Intermittency, stopping and starting.

Urgency.

Weak stream.

Straining to begin.

Nocturia.

A score under eight is mild, eight to nineteen moderate, twenty or higher severe. The pharmacist can use this score to start an alpha-1 antagonist (tamsulosin, alfuzosin), counsel on orthostatic hypotension and intraoperative floppy iris syndrome (relevant before cataract surgery), and monitor over time.

Incontinence types.

Stress incontinence: leakage with cough, sneeze, exertion. First-line is pelvic floor therapy. Topical estrogen in postmenopausal women may help.

Urge incontinence: sudden urgency followed by leakage. Anticholinergics like oxybutynin, tolterodine, solifenacin, fesoterodine, or the beta-3 agonist mirabegron. Mirabegron is preferred in older adults due to anticholinergic burden concerns.

Mixed incontinence: both.

Overflow incontinence: dribbling from a chronically distended bladder, often in BPH or neurogenic bladder.

Kidney Disease: The Body Tells You

Patients with chronic kidney disease have characteristic findings on exam.

Skin: dry, scaly, sometimes hyperpigmented. Pruritus from uremic toxins. Pallor from anemia of CKD. Half-and-half nails: proximal white, distal pink to brown.

Cardiovascular: hypertension, signs of volume overload, sometimes pericardial friction rub in uremia.

Neurologic: encephalopathy in advanced uremia, peripheral neuropathy, asterixis (flapping tremor with arms outstretched and wrists extended).

Musculoskeletal: bone pain from renal osteodystrophy.

Edema: bilateral lower extremity, periorbital in nephrotic-range proteinuria.

Pharmacist relevance. Dose-adjust renally cleared drugs. Avoid nephrotoxic combinations (NSAIDs plus diuretics plus ACE inhibitor, the "triple whammy"). Monitor potassium in patients on RAAS inhibitors, MRA, and trimethoprim-containing antibiotics.

Drug-Induced Urinary Findings

Discolored urine.

Red or red-brown: rifampin, phenazopyridine, some laxatives (senna), rhabdomyolysis (myoglobinuria, looks like blood but dipstick positive without RBCs on micro).

Orange: phenazopyridine, rifampin, some chemotherapy agents, B vitamins in high doses.

Brown or dark amber: dehydration, metronidazole, nitrofurantoin, methyldopa.

Blue or green: amitriptyline, propofol, methylene blue, indomethacin.

Black on standing: methyldopa, methocarbamol, alkaptonuria.

New incontinence on a medication. Alpha-1 antagonists (tamsulosin) relax the urethral sphincter and can worsen stress incontinence in women. SSRIs can cause urinary retention or, paradoxically, urinary frequency. Cholinesterase inhibitors used in dementia can cause urgency and frequency. Lithium causes polyuria from nephrogenic DI.

Hematuria. NSAIDs in interstitial nephritis. Anticoagulants can unmask underlying urinary tract pathology. Cyclophosphamide-induced hemorrhagic cystitis, mitigated with mesna. The "first dose, last visit" rule for hematuria: workup is always indicated; new gross hematuria in an older patient warrants cystoscopy.

Sexual and Reproductive Considerations

Within pharmacist scope, sexual side effects of medications often go unaddressed. Common patterns.

Antidepressants. SSRIs and SNRIs cause anorgasmia, decreased libido, delayed ejaculation. Bupropion, mirtazapine, and vilazodone have lower sexual side effect profiles.

Antihypertensives. Beta-blockers (especially non-selective), thiazides, spironolactone (also gynecomastia), and clonidine. ACE inhibitors and ARBs are relatively neutral.

Antipsychotics. Hyperprolactinemia-related sexual dysfunction. Switch to aripiprazole if problematic.

5-alpha-reductase inhibitors (finasteride, dutasteride). Sexual dysfunction, sometimes persistent.

Opioids. Hypogonadism with chronic use, especially long-acting, presenting with low libido and sometimes osteoporosis.

Pharmacist Pearls

Pearl one. Initiate alpha-1 antagonists at bedtime to minimize first-dose orthostatic hypotension.

Pearl two. Avoid anticholinergic bladder agents in older adults; mirabegron is preferred.

Pearl three. Ask about sexual side effects directly. Patients underreport unless asked.

Pearl four. New CVA tenderness in a diabetic patient on a SGLT2 inhibitor: don't dismiss as routine UTI. Pyelonephritis risk is real.

Red Flags

Acute urinary retention with severe pain. Pyelonephritis with sepsis. Hematuria with weight loss in older adults: bladder cancer. Acute kidney injury manifesting as oliguria, edema, encephalopathy. Anuria. Phimosis or priapism in a patient on PDE5 inhibitor or sickle cell disease.

Audio Quiz

Five questions.

Question one.

A diabetic patient on empagliflozin presents with fever, flank pain, and CVA tenderness on the right side. What is your concern and the action?

The answer: pyelonephritis. SGLT2 inhibitors increase urinary glucose, raising UTI risk and rarely allowing progression to pyelonephritis or even emphysematous infections. Refer for urgent evaluation, urine culture, and consider holding the SGLT2 inhibitor.

Question two.

A 78-year-old man on diphenhydramine for sleep presents with suprapubic distention and inability to void. What is the diagnosis and the lesson?

The answer: acute urinary retention from anticholinergic effect, likely on a background of subclinical BPH. Catheterize to relieve, stop the diphenhydramine, and offer a safer sleep alternative. Avoid anticholinergics in older men.

Question three.

A patient is taking nitrofurantoin for prophylaxis of recurrent UTIs. She reports darker urine and is worried. What is your response?

The answer: nitrofurantoin commonly causes dark brown or amber urine, which is benign. However, if the patient also reports new dyspnea or cough, evaluate for nitrofurantoin pulmonary toxicity. Reassure regarding the color alone.

Question four.

A patient on a tricyclic antidepressant complains of erectile dysfunction. What other antidepressant classes have a lower risk of sexual side effects?

The answer: bupropion, mirtazapine, and vilazodone have lower sexual side-effect profiles than tricyclics or SSRIs.

Question five.

A patient on chronic opioids reports low libido and chronic fatigue. What endocrine consequence should you screen for?

The answer: opioid-induced hypogonadism. Check morning total testosterone in men. In women, ask about menstrual changes. Consider opioid dose reduction or alternative therapies, and refer for endocrine evaluation if confirmed.
