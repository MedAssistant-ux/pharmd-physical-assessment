Episode Seven. Peripheral Vascular and Lymphatic.

Welcome back. The peripheral vascular exam is short and high-yield for pharmacists, especially those who manage anticoagulation, diabetes, and antihypertensives. You will identify peripheral arterial disease that puts your statin and antiplatelet decisions in context, screen for DVT in patients on hormonal therapies, and recognize lymph node patterns that point to infection or malignancy.

Learning Objectives

One. Locate and grade peripheral pulses.

Two. Measure and interpret the ankle-brachial index.

Three. Distinguish arterial from venous insufficiency findings.

Four. Recognize the clinical signs of deep vein thrombosis.

Five. Palpate lymph node chains systematically.

Pulses

Palpate symmetrically: right and left, top to bottom. The pulses to know and where to find them.

Carotid: medial to the sternocleidomastoid, at the level of the cricoid cartilage. Palpate one side at a time, never both at once, to avoid reducing cerebral perfusion.

Brachial: medial to the biceps tendon in the antecubital fossa.

Radial: at the lateral wrist between the radius and the flexor carpi radialis tendon.

Femoral: midway between the anterior superior iliac spine and the pubic symphysis.

Popliteal: deep in the popliteal fossa with the knee slightly flexed.

Posterior tibial: behind and below the medial malleolus.

Dorsalis pedis: on the dorsum of the foot, lateral to the extensor hallucis longus tendon.

Grade pulses on a zero to four scale.

Zero: absent.

One plus: diminished, weak, thready.

Two plus: normal.

Three plus: full, increased.

Four plus: bounding, suggesting aortic regurgitation, hyperthyroidism, or anxiety.

Asymmetric pulses or absent distal pulses suggest peripheral arterial disease, aortic dissection, or embolic occlusion.

The Ankle-Brachial Index

The ABI is a simple, validated tool for diagnosing peripheral arterial disease.

Technique. Take the systolic blood pressure in both arms with a Doppler probe. Take the systolic pressure at the posterior tibial and dorsalis pedis in each ankle with a Doppler. For each leg, divide the higher ankle pressure by the higher arm pressure.

Interpretation.

Greater than 1.4: non-compressible vessels, often from medial calcification in diabetes or chronic kidney disease. Misleadingly high.

1.0 to 1.4: normal.

0.9 to 1.0: borderline.

0.7 to 0.9: mild PAD.

0.4 to 0.7: moderate PAD.

Less than 0.4: severe PAD, with risk of critical limb ischemia.

The pharmacist relevance. A patient with diabetes and intermittent claudication needs an ABI to guide therapy. The patient with established PAD should be on a statin, antiplatelet therapy, smoking cessation support, and supervised exercise. Recognizing the disease drives the pharmacotherapy.

Arterial Insufficiency Signs

The five Ps of acute arterial occlusion: Pain, Pallor, Pulselessness, Paresthesia, Paralysis. Acute occlusion is a surgical emergency.

Chronic arterial insufficiency: cool, pale, hairless distal limbs, atrophic skin, brittle nails, slow capillary refill, dependent rubor that blanches on elevation. Ulcers are typically on the toes, lateral malleoli, or pressure points. They are punched-out, painful, with a dry base, and minimal granulation.

Venous Insufficiency Signs

Chronic venous insufficiency presents very differently. Warm, often edematous lower legs. Brown hemosiderin staining around the medial malleolus. Varicose veins. Stasis dermatitis: scaly, itchy, sometimes weepy patches on the lower legs. Ulcers, if present, are in the gaiter region, the area from mid-calf to ankle, classically over the medial malleolus, with irregular borders, moderate to heavy exudate, and significant granulation tissue.

A practical contrast. Arterial ulcers hurt and look dry. Venous ulcers ooze and are less painful. Both can coexist in patients with mixed disease, and that affects compression therapy decisions, where compression must be cautious or avoided in significant arterial disease.

Deep Vein Thrombosis Assessment

The clinical presentation of DVT: unilateral leg swelling, calf tenderness, warmth, sometimes erythema, sometimes a palpable cord. Often no findings at all, which is why clinical scoring matters.

Wells' criteria for DVT. Each item scores one point unless noted.

Active cancer within the past six months or palliative treatment.

Paralysis, paresis, or recent immobilization of the lower extremity.

Bedridden three or more days, or major surgery within twelve weeks under anesthesia.

Localized tenderness along the deep venous system.

Entire leg swollen.

Calf swelling more than three centimeters compared with the asymptomatic leg, measured ten centimeters below the tibial tuberosity.

Pitting edema confined to the symptomatic leg.

Collateral superficial veins, non-varicose.

Previously documented DVT.

Alternative diagnosis at least as likely as DVT: subtract two points.

Score interpretation. Two or more is DVT likely. Less than two is DVT unlikely. The standard pathway is to combine the score with D-dimer and ultrasound depending on local protocol.

Homans' sign — pain on dorsiflexion — is not reliable. Don't rely on it. Don't elicit it deliberately, as it has poor sensitivity and risks dislodging clot.

Phlegmasia Cerulea Dolens

A massive iliofemoral DVT can produce a swollen, cyanotic, painful leg with compromised arterial inflow. This is a limb-threatening emergency. Refer urgently.

Lymphatic Assessment

Lymph nodes are inspected, palpated, and described by size, tenderness, consistency, mobility, and location.

Major chains to know.

Cervical: covered in episode four.

Supraclavicular: above the clavicle. A palpable supraclavicular node is almost always pathologic. Left side, Virchow's node, suggests gastrointestinal or thoracic malignancy.

Axillary: have the patient relax the arm against your support so the axillary muscles loosen. Palpate the apex, the medial, lateral, anterior, and posterior walls.

Epitrochlear: medial above the elbow. Enlarged in some lymphomas, secondary syphilis, and certain rheumatologic conditions.

Inguinal: along the inguinal ligament. Small, soft, mobile inguinal nodes up to a centimeter are normal in many adults.

Popliteal: posterior to the knee. Almost always abnormal if palpable.

Lymphadenopathy patterns. Generalized: more than two non-contiguous regions. Causes include viral illness (mononucleosis, HIV, CMV), connective tissue disease, lymphoma, leukemia, and certain drugs (phenytoin, hydralazine, sulfonamides, allopurinol). Localized lymphadenopathy: drains the regional infection or malignancy.

Properties. Tender, mobile, soft nodes: usually reactive. Hard, fixed, non-tender, larger than two centimeters, growing over weeks: concerning for malignancy. Refer.

Pharmacist Pearls

Pearl one. A patient on a hormonal contraceptive or postmenopausal hormone therapy who develops a swollen, tender, asymmetric calf: assume DVT until proven otherwise.

Pearl two. Diabetic patients should have annual ABI screening if symptoms or duration of disease warrants. PAD changes the pharmacotherapy: statin, antiplatelet, smoking cessation, often a vasoactive agent.

Pearl three. Compression stockings are first-line for venous insufficiency, but verify adequate arterial supply first. Compression on a patient with significant arterial disease can cause ischemic injury.

Pearl four. A pharmacist who notices a unilateral leg swelling at a routine medication review may be the person who saves that patient from a pulmonary embolism. Stay alert.

Red Flags

Acute cold, pale, pulseless limb. Sudden severe leg pain with cyanosis. Asymmetric leg swelling with risk factors. Hard fixed lymph node growing over weeks. Generalized lymphadenopathy in a young adult with weight loss and night sweats: refer urgently for lymphoma workup.

Audio Quiz

Five questions.

Question one.

A diabetic patient has a measured ABI of 1.5 in the right leg. What does this finding mean?

The answer: non-compressible vessels, likely from medial arterial calcification, common in diabetes and chronic kidney disease. The ABI is falsely elevated and is not interpretable for PAD severity. Consider toe-brachial index or imaging instead.

Question two.

A patient on a combined oral contraceptive returns with a three-centimeter difference in calf circumference compared to the other side, tenderness, and warmth. What is your next step?

The answer: this is high clinical suspicion for DVT. Wells score is likely two or more. Refer for D-dimer testing and lower-extremity ultrasound urgently, and consider stopping the contraceptive once anticoagulation is initiated and a discussion with the prescriber is had.

Question three.

A patient has a leg ulcer over the medial malleolus, surrounded by brown discoloration, with significant exudate and granulation tissue at the base. What kind of ulcer, and what therapy is first-line?

The answer: venous stasis ulcer. First-line therapy is compression therapy, provided arterial supply is adequate, in addition to wound care and leg elevation.

Question four.

A patient is found to have a hard, non-tender, fixed left supraclavicular lymph node, growing over weeks. What is this finding called and what is its significance?

The answer: Virchow's node, classically associated with metastatic abdominal malignancy, particularly gastric, but other primaries also possible. Refer for urgent evaluation.

Question five.

A patient on phenytoin presents with generalized lymphadenopathy involving cervical, axillary, and inguinal regions. What drug-related entity should you consider?

The answer: drug-induced pseudolymphoma or DRESS syndrome from phenytoin. Stop the drug and refer. Aromatic anticonvulsants are classic culprits for hypersensitivity syndromes with prominent lymphadenopathy.
