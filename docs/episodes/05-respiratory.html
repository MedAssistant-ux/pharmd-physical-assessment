Episode Five. The Respiratory Exam.

Welcome back. The respiratory exam is one of the highest-yield exams a pharmacist clinician can perform. Patients on inhaled medications, oral steroids, biologics for asthma, and chronic anticoagulation come in for follow-up visits where a competent pulmonary assessment changes the plan. Today we'll cover the inspection patterns that reveal disease before the stethoscope ever touches the chest, the percussion technique you'll actually use, and the three abnormal breath sounds that anchor your respiratory differentials.

Learning Objectives

One. Inspect the chest and respiratory effort, recognizing patterns of distress and chronic disease.

Two. Palpate for chest expansion and tactile fremitus.

Three. Percuss the lung fields and interpret the resulting sounds.

Four. Auscultate breath sounds and distinguish wheezes, crackles, and rhonchi.

Five. Apply the exam to common pharmacist scenarios: the asthmatic, the COPD exacerbation, and the patient with new dyspnea on an ACE inhibitor.

Inspection: Where Most of the Diagnosis Lives

Expose the chest. Stand back. Watch the patient breathe for at least thirty seconds before you touch them.

Rate and Pattern

Normal: twelve to twenty breaths per minute, regular, with about a one-to-two ratio of inspiration to expiration, and minimal effort.

Tachypnea: over twenty in an adult. The earliest sign of many serious diseases.

Bradypnea: under twelve. Concerning on opioids, sedatives, or in advanced respiratory failure with carbon dioxide retention.

Hyperpnea: deep, often rapid breathing. Classic for diabetic ketoacidosis as Kussmaul respirations, where the body is trying to blow off carbon dioxide to compensate for metabolic acidosis. Also seen in salicylate toxicity.

Cheyne-Stokes respiration: cycles of crescendo and decrescendo breathing with apneic pauses. Seen in heart failure, neurologic injury, and near end of life.

Prolonged expiration: a long, often whistling exhale, classic for obstructive lung disease.

Work of Breathing

Look for signs of increased effort.

Use of accessory muscles: sternocleidomastoid, scalenes, trapezius working visibly. A patient who has to sit forward, hands on knees, to breathe is using the tripod position.

Nasal flaring in adults: a serious sign.

Intercostal retractions: skin sucking in between the ribs with each breath. Common in pediatric distress and severe adult disease.

Pursed-lip breathing: characteristic of COPD patients who instinctively create back-pressure to keep small airways open.

Audible wheeze without a stethoscope: severe airflow obstruction.

The inability to complete a sentence in one breath: a major sign of respiratory distress. A patient who can answer "yes" but not "I have been feeling worse since Sunday" is in trouble.

Chest Shape

A normal adult chest has an anteroposterior-to-lateral diameter ratio of about one to two. A barrel chest, where the ratio approaches one to one, suggests hyperinflation from COPD.

Pectus excavatum: a sunken sternum, often benign cosmetic but can compromise cardiac filling in severe cases.

Pectus carinatum: protruding sternum.

Kyphosis and scoliosis of the thoracic spine: can restrict lung volumes.

Cyanosis and Clubbing

Cyanosis of the lips and tongue, called central cyanosis, suggests true hypoxemia. Cyanosis of the fingers only, called peripheral cyanosis, may just be poor perfusion or cold exposure. Clubbing of the fingers, as we covered last episode, points to chronic hypoxic lung disease or other long-standing systemic conditions.

Palpation

Chest Expansion

Place your hands on the patient's back at the level of the tenth ribs, thumbs touching at the spine, fingers spread. Ask the patient to take a deep breath. Your thumbs should move equally apart by two to three centimeters.

Asymmetric expansion: the side that moves less is the abnormal side. Consider pleural effusion, pneumothorax, lobar collapse, or consolidation.

Tactile Fremitus

Place the ulnar surface of your hands or the palm on the patient's back. Have the patient repeat "ninety-nine" in a low voice. You feel the vibration through the chest wall.

Increased fremitus: dense, fluid- or pus-filled tissue conducts vibration better. Think consolidation as in pneumonia.

Decreased fremitus: air or fluid in the pleural space dampens vibration. Think pleural effusion, pneumothorax, hyperinflation in COPD.

Percussion

Percuss systematically. Apex to base, side to side, comparing left and right at each level. Two or three taps at each spot. The expected sound over normal lung is resonant: a hollow, full sound.

Dull percussion: solid or fluid replaces air. Pneumonia, pleural effusion, hemothorax, lung mass, lobar collapse.

Hyperresonant percussion: more air than normal. Pneumothorax, COPD with hyperinflation.

Flat percussion: extremely solid. Over a massive consolidation or a large effusion.

A pharmacist tip. You probably won't percuss every patient. But when the auscultation is ambiguous, three taps on each side can help you decide whether you're hearing fluid, air, or normal lung.

Auscultation

Use the diaphragm of the stethoscope. Skin contact, not over clothing. Have the patient breathe in and out through an open mouth, deeper than usual. Listen at apex, mid-zone, and base, both anterior and posterior, comparing side to side.

Normal Breath Sounds

Vesicular: soft, low-pitched, heard over most of the lung fields. Inspiration is longer than expiration. This is the dominant normal sound.

Bronchovesicular: medium pitch, heard over the main bronchi, between the scapulae posteriorly and around the sternum anteriorly. Inspiration and expiration roughly equal.

Bronchial or tubular: loud, high-pitched, heard over the trachea. Expiration longer than inspiration. If you hear bronchial breath sounds out in the lung periphery, it's a sign of consolidation that is conducting sound from the airways more efficiently.

Adventitious Sounds: The Three You Must Know

Wheezes. High-pitched, musical, whistling. Usually heard on expiration but can be inspiratory too. Caused by narrowed airways. Diffuse wheezing: asthma exacerbation, COPD exacerbation, anaphylaxis. Focal wheezing in one location: foreign body, tumor, or focal bronchospasm.

Crackles, also called rales. Discontinuous, short, popping sounds, like rubbing hair between your fingers next to your ear. Caused by sudden opening of small airways or by fluid in alveoli. Fine crackles at the bases bilaterally: pulmonary edema from heart failure. Coarse crackles, often localized: pneumonia. Velcro-like crackles at the bases: pulmonary fibrosis, including drug-induced fibrosis from amiodarone, methotrexate, nitrofurantoin, and bleomycin.

Rhonchi. Low-pitched, snoring or gurgling sounds, often clearing with cough. They reflect secretions in larger airways. Bronchitis, COPD with secretions.

A fourth, less common but important.

Pleural friction rub. A creaky, grating sound, like leather rubbing leather, heard with both inspiration and expiration in the same location, not affected by cough. Indicates inflamed pleural surfaces rubbing together, as in pleurisy, pulmonary embolism, or pleural infection.

Vocal Sounds for Consolidation

When you suspect consolidation, three bedside tests help confirm.

Bronchophony: have the patient say "ninety-nine" in a normal voice. Over normal lung, the words sound muffled. Over consolidated lung, they sound clearer and louder.

Whispered pectoriloquy: patient whispers "one, two, three." Whispers are normally indistinct. Over consolidation, the whisper is clearly audible.

Egophony: patient says "E" continuously. Over consolidated lung, it sounds like "A." The classic E-to-A change.

These are simple, fast, and useful at the bedside.

Applying the Exam: The Pharmacist's Scenarios

The Asthma Visit

What you do at every asthma visit:

Inspect for use of accessory muscles, ability to speak in full sentences, and posture. Tripod, can't finish sentences? Severe.

Listen to all lung fields for wheezing. Note whether wheezing is on inspiration, expiration, or both. Inspiratory plus expiratory wheezing in a patient who is silent on auscultation, the so-called "silent chest," is a critical sign of impending respiratory failure. The chest is silent because no air is moving.

Measure peak expiratory flow if available. Compare to the patient's personal best. Less than fifty percent of personal best is severe.

Have the patient demonstrate inhaler technique. Most patients use their inhalers wrong. This is the single highest-yield intervention a pharmacist makes in asthma care.

The COPD Exacerbation

Look for: barrel chest, pursed-lip breathing, accessory muscle use, central cyanosis. Auscultate for prolonged expiration, wheezes, and rhonchi.

Hallmarks of exacerbation: increase in dyspnea, increase in sputum volume, increase in sputum purulence — the so-called Anthonisen criteria. Two or three of these warrant antibiotics in addition to bronchodilators and a short course of oral corticosteroids.

Pharmacist pearls in COPD: long-acting maintenance inhalers should not be used as rescue. Short-acting albuterol is rescue. ICS-containing combination inhalers reduce exacerbations in selected patients but increase pneumonia risk; identify patients with frequent exacerbations and elevated eosinophils who benefit most.

The ACE Inhibitor Cough

Up to twenty percent of patients on an ACE inhibitor develop a dry, persistent cough, typically within weeks but sometimes months after starting therapy. It is non-productive, tickly, often worse at night.

The exam: typically the lung exam is clean. No wheeze, no crackles, no consolidation findings. A patient with a clean lung exam but a persistent dry cough on an ACE inhibitor: stop the ACE inhibitor and switch to an ARB. The cough usually resolves within four weeks.

If the exam is not clean, look elsewhere. Crackles? Heart failure, pneumonia. Wheezes? Asthma. New cough plus dyspnea plus crackles plus a patient on amiodarone? Think amiodarone pulmonary toxicity, a serious adverse effect that can present as bilateral basal fibrosis.

The Patient on Methotrexate Who Reports a Dry Cough

Methotrexate pneumonitis is an idiosyncratic, potentially fatal lung reaction. Subacute onset of dry cough, dyspnea, and sometimes fever. The exam may show fine inspiratory crackles. Stop the methotrexate immediately and refer for evaluation. The same caution applies to nitrofurantoin and bleomycin lung toxicity.

Pharmacist Pearls

Pearl one. The silent chest in asthma is not "getting better." It is impending respiratory failure. Recognize and refer immediately.

Pearl two. Bilateral basal fine inspiratory crackles in a patient on amiodarone, methotrexate, nitrofurantoin, or bleomycin: think drug-induced interstitial lung disease. Stop the drug and refer.

Pearl three. Every asthma and COPD visit should include an inhaler technique demonstration. The medication is only as effective as the technique.

Pearl four. A patient on an ACE inhibitor with a dry cough and a normal lung exam: the cough is the diagnosis, not pneumonia. Switch to ARB.

Red Flags

Silent chest in asthma. Inability to complete a sentence. Cyanosis. Respiratory rate over thirty. Use of accessory muscles plus drowsiness. SpO2 below ninety on room air. Sudden pleuritic chest pain plus dyspnea, especially in a patient on hormonal contraception or with malignancy: think pulmonary embolism. Hemoptysis with weight loss in a long-term smoker: refer urgently.

Audio Quiz

Five questions.

Question one.

A patient on lisinopril for one month presents with a persistent dry cough and a normal lung exam. What is the most likely diagnosis and the management?

The answer: ACE inhibitor-induced cough. The lung exam is typically clean. Stop the lisinopril and switch to an ARB like losartan. The cough usually resolves within four weeks.

Question two.

An asthmatic teenager presents in severe distress. He cannot complete a sentence, is using accessory muscles, and on auscultation there is no wheezing audible. What does this finding suggest?

The answer: silent chest, indicating critically reduced airflow and impending respiratory failure. He needs immediate aggressive bronchodilator therapy, systemic corticosteroids, and emergency department transport. Do not interpret quiet lungs as improvement.

Question three.

A patient on amiodarone for two years reports new dyspnea on exertion and a dry cough. On exam, you hear fine velcro-like crackles at both lung bases. What is the concern and what is the action?

The answer: amiodarone pulmonary toxicity, possibly interstitial pneumonitis or fibrosis. Stop the amiodarone in consultation with the prescriber, order a chest imaging study and pulmonary function tests, and refer to pulmonology.

Question four.

A COPD patient presents with increased dyspnea, increased sputum volume, and a change in sputum from white to yellow. What treatment is supported by the Anthonisen criteria?

The answer: antibiotics, a short course of oral corticosteroids, and intensified bronchodilator therapy. The patient has three of three Anthonisen criteria, the threshold for antibiotic-responsive bacterial exacerbation.

Question five.

A patient says "ninety-nine" and over the right lower lung field the words are louder and clearer than over the left lower lung. Egophony is present. What does this suggest?

The answer: consolidation in the right lower lobe. Bronchophony and egophony, the E-to-A change, are signs that sound is being transmitted through dense alveoli filled with fluid or pus, classically pneumonia.
