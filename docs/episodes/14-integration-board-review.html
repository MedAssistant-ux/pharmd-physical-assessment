Episode Fourteen. Integration and Board-Style Review.

Welcome to the final episode. The first thirteen episodes taught the techniques and the body systems. This episode integrates them into the actual visits a pharmacist clinician performs, and then closes with twenty board-style review questions read aloud.

Part One: Focused Assessment Templates

For each common ambulatory care visit, here is the focused exam you should perform and the documentation you should produce.

Hypertension Follow-Up

Subjective. Adherence to current regimen. Side effects: dizziness, cough, edema, sexual dysfunction. Home blood pressure readings. Dietary salt intake. Alcohol. Other recent medication changes including over the counter NSAIDs and decongestants.

Objective. Vital signs with technique done correctly. Orthostatic vitals if dose was titrated up or symptoms suggest. Heart rate and rhythm. Auscultation of lungs for crackles. Inspection of legs for edema. PMI for sustained impulse suggesting LVH.

Assessment. Hypertension, state controlled or not, and likely contributors.

Plan. Specific medication change with dose, monitoring plan, follow-up timing, and contingency for hypertensive urgency.

Diabetes Visit

Subjective. Hypoglycemia frequency and severity. Symptoms suggesting hyper- or hypoglycemia. Adherence to lifestyle and medications. Sick day management understanding.

Objective. Vital signs. Weight, BMI, waist circumference. Diabetic foot exam: visual inspection, pulses, monofilament, vibration, reflexes. Skin: acanthosis nigricans, infections, injection sites. Eyes: ask about screening exam date.

Assessment. Type 2 diabetes, controlled or not by A1c and time in range. Identify barriers.

Plan. Adjustments to therapy, prevention of complications, screening updates, follow-up.

Asthma or COPD Visit

Subjective. Rescue inhaler use frequency. Nighttime symptoms. Exercise tolerance. Exacerbations and ER visits. Trigger identification. Adherence.

Objective. Vital signs including SpO2. Inspection for accessory muscle use and chest shape. Auscultation for wheezes, crackles, rhonchi. Peak expiratory flow if available. Inhaler technique demonstration.

Assessment. Severity of asthma, control level by GINA or NAEPP criteria. For COPD, GOLD group assignment.

Plan. Step up or step down therapy. Counsel on technique, action plan, vaccinations.

Anticoagulation Visit

Subjective. Bleeding (gum, nasal, urinary, stool, bruising). New medications including OTC. Falls. Pregnancy. Dental procedures planned.

Objective. Vital signs. Skin: bruising, petechiae, hematomas. INR if on warfarin. Renal function for DOAC dosing.

Assessment. INR in range or not (for warfarin). Bleeding or thrombotic events.

Plan. Dose adjustment. Counseling. Refill duration.

Heart Failure Visit

Subjective. NYHA class. Weight changes. Orthopnea (pillow count). Paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnea. Diuretic adherence. Salt and fluid restriction.

Objective. Vital signs including orthostatics. Weight, comparing to baseline. JVP estimation. Auscultation for S3 and basal crackles. Inspection of legs for edema. Hepatojugular reflux if uncertain.

Assessment. HFrEF or HFpEF, NYHA class, volume status (euvolemic, hypervolemic, hypovolemic).

Plan. Goal-directed medical therapy titration, diuretic adjustment, education, follow-up.

Geriatric Polypharmacy Visit

Subjective. Falls, cognitive changes, ADL function, sleep, mood, pain, urinary symptoms.

Objective. Vital signs with orthostatics. Mini-Cog or other cognitive screen. Timed Up and Go. Inspection of pill organizer if available.

Assessment. Identify potentially inappropriate medications, prescribing cascades, adherence barriers.

Plan. Deprescribe one or two non-essential medications per visit. Continue routine ones with re-evaluation built in.

Part Two: Board-Style Review

Twenty questions. Same format as before: question, five-second pause, answer with rationale.

Question one.

A patient on lisinopril and hydrochlorothiazide is referred for medication review. Standing systolic blood pressure is 28 millimeters of mercury lower than supine, and standing heart rate increases by 22 beats per minute. The patient reports lightheadedness on rising. What is the diagnosis and likely contributor?

The answer: orthostatic hypotension with appropriate compensatory tachycardia, suggesting volume depletion. The thiazide is the likely contributor. Consider reducing or holding the diuretic, ensure adequate intake, and recheck.

Question two.

A pharmacist auscultates a holosystolic, blowing murmur at the apex radiating to the axilla in a 76-year-old patient with new dyspnea. What valve lesion, and what initial step?

The answer: mitral regurgitation. Refer for transthoracic echocardiography to grade severity and guide management. Initiate appropriate symptom-directed therapy under prescriber guidance.

Question three.

A patient on simvastatin 80 mg is prescribed clarithromycin for sinusitis. He returns three days later with severe muscle pain and dark urine. What is the management priority?

The answer: stop both medications, check creatine kinase, electrolytes, and renal function, initiate aggressive intravenous hydration to prevent acute kidney injury from rhabdomyolysis, and refer. Clarithromycin is a strong CYP3A4 inhibitor and dramatically increases simvastatin exposure.

Question four.

A 30-year-old woman three weeks into lamotrigine therapy presents with fever, facial swelling, a diffuse morbilliform rash, and an AST that is three times upper limit of normal. What is the diagnosis?

The answer: DRESS syndrome. Stop lamotrigine immediately and refer. Lamotrigine is a classic culprit.

Question five.

A patient with peripheral arterial disease has a venous stasis ulcer on the medial malleolus. Compression therapy is being considered. What ABI threshold makes compression safe?

The answer: ABI of 0.8 or higher is generally considered safe for full compression. ABI below 0.5 contraindicates compression. The intermediate range requires modified compression with vascular consultation.

Question six.

A 4-year-old with viral illness is given aspirin by a well-meaning grandparent. He develops vomiting and altered mental status the next day. What syndrome must be considered, and what is the lesson?

The answer: Reye's syndrome. Aspirin must not be given to children with viral illness due to the risk of acute encephalopathy and hepatic failure. Counsel families at every pediatric encounter.

Question seven.

A patient on a SGLT2 inhibitor presents with malaise, normal blood glucose, ketones in the urine, and an anion gap. What complication should be considered?

The answer: euglycemic diabetic ketoacidosis. SGLT2 inhibitors can precipitate DKA without significant hyperglycemia, especially in the setting of illness, fasting, or surgery. Stop the medication, treat the DKA, and counsel on sick-day management.

Question eight.

A patient on amiodarone presents with new fine basal crackles bilaterally and a dry cough. What complication and what action?

The answer: amiodarone pulmonary toxicity, with possible pneumonitis or fibrosis. Stop amiodarone in consultation with the prescriber, obtain imaging and pulmonary function tests, and refer to pulmonology.

Question nine.

A patient on linezolid is also taking citalopram and is given tramadol for an injury. What syndrome must you anticipate?

The answer: serotonin syndrome. Linezolid is a weak MAOI, citalopram is an SSRI, and tramadol has serotonergic activity. The combination can produce serotonin syndrome with hyperthermia, clonus, agitation, and autonomic instability. Avoid these combinations.

Question ten.

A 67-year-old smoker presents with new clubbing of his fingers and weight loss. What underlying condition should be excluded?

The answer: lung malignancy must be excluded. New clubbing in an adult, especially a smoker, is concerning and warrants chest imaging.

Question eleven.

A patient on chronic prednisone develops sudden right groin pain and limited internal rotation of the hip. What complication?

The answer: avascular necrosis of the femoral head. MRI is the most sensitive imaging study. Refer for orthopedic evaluation.

Question twelve.

A patient on hydroxychloroquine for five years is overdue for an ophthalmologic exam. What screening is required, and why?

The answer: baseline ophthalmologic exam and annual screening after five years of hydroxychloroquine therapy. The toxicity is retinopathy, which is irreversible but largely preventable with adherence to screening.

Question thirteen.

A new patient on warfarin develops bilateral painful red-purple skin lesions on her thighs three days after starting therapy. What is the diagnosis?

The answer: warfarin-induced skin necrosis. Stop warfarin, treat with alternative anticoagulation, and consider underlying protein C or S deficiency. This is uncommon but classic.

Question fourteen.

A patient on chronic opioids reports fatigue, low libido, and erectile dysfunction. What endocrine complication?

The answer: opioid-induced hypogonadism. Check morning testosterone. Consider dose reduction or alternative therapy. Counsel on bone health.

Question fifteen.

A patient on warfarin and amiodarone has an INR of 7. What interaction explains this?

The answer: amiodarone inhibits CYP2C9, increasing warfarin exposure and INR. Reduce warfarin by approximately 30 to 50 percent when starting amiodarone, and monitor INR closely.

Question sixteen.

A patient with chronic kidney disease and gout is being considered for febuxostat. What boxed warning should you consider?

The answer: cardiovascular death risk warning. Use febuxostat in patients who cannot tolerate or fail allopurinol, weighing benefits and risks.

Question seventeen.

A patient on metformin presents with new dyspnea, lethargy, and abdominal pain after a contrast CT scan. Lactate is high. What is the concern?

The answer: metformin-associated lactic acidosis, precipitated by contrast-induced acute kidney injury. Metformin should be held before contrast in patients at risk and resumed after renal function is reassessed.

Question eighteen.

An 80-year-old patient on diphenhydramine, oxybutynin, and lorazepam at bedtime develops a fall. What pharmacist intervention is highest impact?

The answer: deprescribe. Each of these contributes to fall risk through anticholinergic and sedating effects. Replace with safer alternatives or non-pharmacologic strategies.

Question nineteen.

A patient on a daily proton pump inhibitor for five years asks about long-term risks. What complications should you discuss?

The answer: long-term PPI use is associated with B12 deficiency, hypomagnesemia, increased fracture risk, C. difficile colitis risk, and possible increase in chronic kidney disease. Discuss whether the indication is still valid and consider step-down therapy.

Question twenty.

A patient on a TNF-alpha inhibitor presents with new cough and weight loss. What latent infection should be considered?

The answer: reactivation tuberculosis. TNF inhibitors carry a risk of TB reactivation. Patients should be screened with a tuberculin skin test or interferon-gamma release assay before initiation, and any new respiratory symptoms warrant evaluation.

Final Pearls

Five pearls to carry forward.

One. The exam is always tied to the medication. If you cannot articulate why a physical finding matters for pharmacotherapy, you are doing the wrong exam.

Two. Document objective findings — including normal ones — to make your reasoning auditable.

Three. Trends matter more than single readings. Track over time.

Four. Recognize when you are outside your scope and refer with confidence. Knowing your limits is not a weakness; it is the foundation of safe practice.

Five. Practice the exam every day on every patient, even at the counseling counter. Skill comes from repetition.

Course Wrap-Up

You have completed the PharmD Physical Assessment Audio Course. Fourteen episodes. The four exam techniques. The major body systems. The drug correlates that make this exam the pharmacist's exam.

The next step is practice. Find a willing partner. Practice taking blood pressure correctly. Practice palpating thyroids. Practice listening to heart sounds at the four locations and identifying S1 and S2 by carotid-pulse timing. Practice the diabetic foot exam. Practice the Mini-Cog. Each technique becomes automatic only through repetition.

In your clinical practice, integrate the exam into every patient interaction. The pharmacist who can perform a competent focused physical assessment is a high-value clinician on any team.

Good luck on your exam. Good luck in your practice. End of course.
