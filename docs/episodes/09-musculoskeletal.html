Episode Nine. The Musculoskeletal Exam.

Welcome back. The musculoskeletal exam is central to pharmacist practice because so many drugs we manage affect muscles, joints, and bone. Statins cause myopathy. Fluoroquinolones cause tendon rupture. Corticosteroids cause osteoporosis and avascular necrosis. Bisphosphonates can cause atypical fractures. Aromatase inhibitors cause arthralgias. A pharmacist who can perform a basic musculoskeletal exam can distinguish these adverse effects from primary disease.

Learning Objectives

One. Inspect joints for the patterns of disease.

Two. Test active and passive range of motion.

Three. Examine the major joints in a focused way.

Four. Assess gait and posture.

Five. Recognize the drug-induced musculoskeletal entities.

General Principles

For any joint, follow this sequence: inspect, palpate, range of motion (active first, then passive), assess strength against resistance, and apply provocative tests as indicated.

Inspect for swelling, redness, deformity, and muscle atrophy. Compare side to side. Asymmetry is the most useful early clue.

Palpate for warmth, tenderness, effusion, and crepitus. A warm joint with effusion in a single distal joint of an older adult: think gout or septic joint — both worth ruling out. A warm joint in multiple small joints symmetrically: think inflammatory arthritis.

Range of motion. Active ROM is the patient moving the joint. Passive ROM is you moving it. A patient who has limited active ROM but full passive ROM has weakness or pain inhibition, not a mechanical block. A patient with limited active and passive ROM has a true joint or capsule problem.

Joint Pattern Recognition

The pattern of joint involvement narrows the differential.

Monoarticular: gout, septic joint, trauma, hemarthrosis on anticoagulant.

Oligoarticular (two to four joints): reactive arthritis, psoriatic arthritis, spondyloarthropathies.

Polyarticular symmetric small joints: rheumatoid arthritis, lupus.

Polyarticular DIP joints with bony nodes: osteoarthritis (Heberden's nodes at DIPs, Bouchard's at PIPs).

Monoarticular acute red hot tender first MTP in a middle-aged man: classic gout, often after diuretic initiation, after a starch-rich meal with alcohol, or after a fasting state.

Examining Major Joints

Shoulder

Inspect from front and back. Palpate the acromioclavicular joint, the bicipital groove, and the rotator cuff insertion points.

Range of motion. Forward flexion to 180 degrees, extension to 60, abduction to 180, internal and external rotation about 90 each.

Drop arm test: passively abduct the arm to 90 degrees. Have the patient slowly lower it. Sudden drop suggests rotator cuff tear.

Neer's and Hawkins-Kennedy tests: impingement signs.

Elbow

Inspect for the carrying angle, normally five to fifteen degrees, more in women. Palpate the olecranon for bursitis. Palpate the lateral epicondyle for tennis elbow tenderness, and the medial epicondyle for golfer's elbow.

Range of motion. Extension to zero, flexion to 150, supination and pronation about 80 each.

Wrist and Hand

Inspect for swelling at the MCP, PIP, and DIP joints. Rheumatoid arthritis classically affects MCP and PIP, sparing DIP. Osteoarthritis affects DIP (Heberden's) and PIP (Bouchard's). Psoriatic arthritis can affect the DIP.

Phalen's test: hold the wrists in flexion for sixty seconds. Tingling in the median nerve distribution suggests carpal tunnel syndrome.

Tinel's test: tap over the median nerve at the wrist. Tingling distally is also suggestive.

Finkelstein's test: thumb tucked in fist, ulnar deviation of the wrist elicits pain in De Quervain's tenosynovitis.

Hip

Inspect for pelvic tilt, leg length discrepancy, atrophy of the gluteal muscles.

Range of motion. Flexion to 120 with knee bent, extension to 30, abduction to 45, internal and external rotation about 40.

FABER test: Flexion, ABduction, External Rotation. Place the heel of the affected leg on the opposite knee. Pain in the groin suggests hip pathology. Pain in the sacroiliac joint suggests sacroiliitis.

Trendelenburg test: have the patient stand on one leg. Drop of the contralateral pelvis indicates weakness of the abductors on the standing leg.

Knee

Inspect for swelling, alignment (varus or valgus), and quadriceps atrophy.

Effusion: with the patient supine, milk fluid from the suprapatellar pouch downward, then press the patella against the femur. A palpable ballotment indicates effusion.

McMurray's test: meniscal injury.

Lachman's test: anterior cruciate ligament injury.

Drawer tests: ACL and PCL.

Varus and valgus stress: collateral ligaments.

Ankle and Foot

Inspect for swelling, deformity, ulcers, and skin changes. Palpate the Achilles tendon. A palpable defect with a positive Thompson's test — squeezing the calf, normally producing plantar flexion — suggests Achilles rupture, a key fluoroquinolone-induced complication.

Range of motion. Dorsiflexion to 20, plantar flexion to 50, inversion and eversion as relevant.

Examine the diabetic foot: visual inspection between the toes, palpation of pulses, monofilament testing for protective sensation, tuning fork for vibration sense. Pharmacist relevance: insulin-treated and uncontrolled diabetic patients need an annual diabetic foot exam.

Spine

Inspect from the back for symmetry and curvature. Lateral curvature is scoliosis. Excess thoracic kyphosis ("dowager's hump") is common with osteoporotic vertebral fractures.

Range of motion. Forward flexion (touch toes), extension (lean back), lateral bending, rotation.

Straight leg raise: with the patient supine, raise the symptomatic leg straight. Pain radiating below the knee at 30 to 70 degrees suggests lumbosacral nerve root compression.

Gait Analysis

Have the patient walk normally, then on toes, then on heels, then in tandem (heel-to-toe).

Antalgic gait: shortened stance phase on the painful side.

Trendelenburg gait: hip drops on the unsupported side, indicating weakness of the contralateral abductors.

Steppage gait: high lifting of the affected leg to clear the foot from foot drop, suggesting peroneal nerve injury.

Shuffling gait: small steps, reduced arm swing — Parkinson's disease, including drug-induced parkinsonism from antipsychotics and metoclopramide.

Wide-based ataxic gait: cerebellar dysfunction. Chronic phenytoin toxicity is a classic drug cause.

Spastic gait: scissoring or stiff-legged — upper motor neuron lesion.

Drug-Induced Musculoskeletal Findings

Statin-associated muscle symptoms. Three patterns. Myalgia: muscle aches without significant CK elevation. Myopathy: aches with CK elevation up to 10 times upper limit of normal. Rhabdomyolysis: severe weakness, very high CK, sometimes red-brown urine, risk of acute kidney injury. Risk factors: high-dose statin, simvastatin specifically, drug interactions (CYP3A4 inhibitors like clarithromycin, OATP1B1 inhibitors like cyclosporine), hypothyroidism, vitamin D deficiency, advanced age, female sex. Exam: tenderness and weakness of proximal muscles. Action: check CK, stop the statin if significant, rechallenge at lower dose or alternative after recovery.

Fluoroquinolone tendinopathy. Achilles tendon most common, can rupture without warning. Risk factors: concomitant corticosteroid, age over sixty, renal impairment, organ transplant. Symptom: acute posterior ankle pain, sometimes with palpable defect and positive Thompson's test. Action: stop the fluoroquinolone, immobilize, refer.

Corticosteroid complications. Osteoporosis: with chronic use of more than 5 mg prednisone daily for more than three months. Avascular necrosis, especially of the femoral head: groin or hip pain, eventually limited range of motion, especially internal rotation. Myopathy: proximal weakness with normal or only modestly elevated CK. Bisphosphonates and other agents reduce fracture risk.

Bisphosphonate atypical femoral fractures: prodrome of thigh or groin pain, can become a complete subtrochanteric fracture. Risk increases beyond five years of use. Counsel patients on long-term bisphosphonates to report unexplained thigh pain.

Aromatase inhibitor arthralgias: bilateral joint pain, often hands and wrists, sometimes severe enough to discontinue. Switching between aromatase inhibitors or to tamoxifen can help.

Diuretics and gout: thiazides and loop diuretics raise serum urate. New gout flare in a patient recently started on a diuretic should prompt a discussion of alternatives.

Pharmacist Pearls

Pearl one. New proximal muscle aches in a statin patient: think statin-associated muscle symptoms, but also rule out hypothyroidism, vitamin D deficiency, and interacting medications.

Pearl two. A patient on chronic prednisone who develops groin pain: think avascular necrosis. Refer for imaging.

Pearl three. An elderly patient on a fluoroquinolone with corticosteroid co-therapy and new heel pain: assume Achilles tendinopathy until proven otherwise. Stop the FQ.

Pearl four. A patient on a bisphosphonate for more than five years with new thigh pain: get a femoral X-ray. Atypical fractures are visible early.

Red Flags

Acute red, hot, swollen single joint: septic arthritis must be ruled out, requires arthrocentesis. Inability to bear weight after a fall in an osteoporosis patient: assume fracture. New back pain in a patient with cancer history: bone metastasis or pathologic fracture. Saddle anesthesia and bowel or bladder dysfunction with low back pain: cauda equina syndrome — emergency.

Audio Quiz

Five questions.

Question one.

A patient on simvastatin 40 mg daily started clarithromycin a week ago and now has severe diffuse muscle pain and tea-colored urine. What is the likely diagnosis and the mechanism?

The answer: rhabdomyolysis from a clinically significant interaction. Clarithromycin is a strong CYP3A4 inhibitor that increases simvastatin exposure substantially. Stop the simvastatin and the macrolide if possible, check CK, electrolytes, and renal function, hydrate aggressively, and refer.

Question two.

A 67-year-old patient on prednisone is started on ciprofloxacin and reports a sudden snap and pain in his calf two days later, with difficulty plantar flexing. What is the likely diagnosis?

The answer: Achilles tendon rupture from fluoroquinolone tendinopathy, amplified by corticosteroid co-therapy. Stop the fluoroquinolone, immobilize, refer urgently for orthopedic evaluation.

Question three.

A patient on alendronate for eight years presents with several weeks of vague right thigh pain that has gradually worsened. What complication should you rule out?

The answer: atypical subtrochanteric or diaphyseal femur fracture. Long-term bisphosphonates increase this risk. Get femoral X-rays and consider a drug holiday after consultation.

Question four.

A patient with rheumatoid arthritis presents with bilateral, symmetric, swollen MCP and PIP joints, with morning stiffness lasting two hours. What pattern, and what disease does it suggest?

The answer: polyarticular symmetric small joint involvement of MCPs and PIPs with prolonged morning stiffness. Classic for rheumatoid arthritis. OA spares MCPs and prefers DIPs; the morning stiffness is shorter in OA.

Question five.

A patient on metoclopramide for chronic gastroparesis develops a shuffling gait, masked facies, and a resting tremor. What is the syndrome and management?

The answer: drug-induced parkinsonism from dopamine receptor blockade. Stop the metoclopramide. Symptoms typically resolve over weeks to months. Consider alternatives for gastroparesis such as low-dose erythromycin.
